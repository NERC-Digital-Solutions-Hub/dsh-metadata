### Susceptibility (NFVI Characteristic)

**Description:** 
This field represents one of the five core characteristics that make up the Neighbourhood Flood Vulnerability Index (NFVI). It describes the predisposition of an individual or community to experience a loss of well-being when exposed to a flood event. 

**Vulnerability Rationale / Components:** 
A higher score in this index indicates a higher susceptibility to harm. Evidence strongly suggests that the dominant personal characteristics influencing a person's susceptibility to the impacts of extreme weather events are their age and health status. Therefore, this characteristic index is constructed by combining indicators across two main domains:
*   **Age:** This domain accounts for the very young and the elderly, who are highly sensitive to the physical and psychological impacts of flooding. It incorporates two specific supporting variables: the percentage of young children (under 5 years old) and the percentage of older people (over 75 years old) in the population.
*   **Health:** This domain accounts for individuals whose pre-existing health conditions make them more vulnerable to the adverse direct and indirect effects of a flood. It incorporates two supporting variables: the percentage of people with a disability or ill-health whose day-to-day activities are limited, and the percentage of households containing at least one person with a long-term limiting illness.

**Data Source and Calculation:** 
The Susceptibility characteristic score is calculated by taking the data for the underlying age and health indicators, transforming them into *z-scores*, and summing them together assuming an equal weighting. The foundational demographic and health data is primarily sourced from the 2011 UK Census and processed as part of the 2017 flood vulnerability assessment by Sayers and Partners for the Joseph Rowntree Foundation. The final data is mapped at the neighbourhood level, corresponding to Lower Super Output Areas (LSOAs) in England and Wales, Data Zones (DZs) in Scotland, and Super Output Areas (SOAs) in Northern Ireland.
