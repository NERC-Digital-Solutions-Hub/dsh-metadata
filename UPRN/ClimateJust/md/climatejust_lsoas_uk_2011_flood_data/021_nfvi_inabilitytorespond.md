### Inability to Respond (Ability to Respond Characteristic)

**Description:** 
This field represents one of the five core characteristics that make up the Neighbourhood Flood Vulnerability Index (NFVI). It measures the personal, environmental, and social factors that explain the underlying reasons why some individuals or communities act more or less effectively in the immediate run-up to and during a flood event. 

**Vulnerability Rationale / Components:** 
A higher score in this index indicates a higher *inability* (and thus greater social vulnerability) to respond to a flood. A community's ability to respond to an extreme event is influenced by a combination of indicators across five main domains:
*   **Income:** Financial deprivation restricts a household's resources and choices during a crisis, such as affording immediate emergency transport or temporary accommodation.
*   **Information Use:** Language barriers (such as low English proficiency) and being a recent arrival to the UK severely reduce a person's ability to access, understand, and interpret official flood warnings and emergency guidance.
*   **Local Knowledge:** New migrants to a local area are highly vulnerable as they lack the historical context, community experience, and awareness of what to do or where to go if their new neighborhood floods.
*   **Physical Mobility:** High levels of disability, living in medical or care establishments, or lacking access to private transport creates significant practical barriers to safely evacuating or moving possessions to higher ground.
*   **Crime:** In areas with high crime rates, residents are often reluctant or refuse to evacuate their properties during a flood for fear of looting, or may hesitate to deploy external flood defenses (like floodgates) because it signals the house is empty.

**Data Source and Calculation:** 
The characteristic index is calculated by transforming the underlying variables for the five domains into *z-scores* and summing them assuming an equal weighting. The foundational data is largely derived from the 2011 UK Census (Office for National Statistics), combined with national crime statistics and the 2017 assessment by Sayers and Partners. The data is mapped down to the neighbourhood level, which corresponds to Lower Super Output Areas (LSOAs) in England and Wales, Data Zones (DZs) in Scotland, and Super Output Areas (SOAs) in Northern Ireland.
