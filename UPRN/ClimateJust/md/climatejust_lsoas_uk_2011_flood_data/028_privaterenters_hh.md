### Private renters (% Households)

**Description:** 
This field represents the percentage of households in a given area that rent their accommodation from a private landlord or letting agency. It serves as a supporting variable for the "Property tenure" indicator, which is used to assess a community's "Ability to Prepare" characteristic within the Neighbourhood Flood Vulnerability Index (NFVI).

**Vulnerability Rationale:** 
A higher proportion of people renting their homes from private landlords indicates a higher level of social vulnerability to flooding. Tenants are generally more vulnerable because they have less capacity than homeowners to adapt their properties and prepare for extreme weather events. The inclusion of this metric is based on several key factors:
* **Restrictions on Modifications:** Tenants are often not permitted by their lease agreements to make physical alterations or install permanent flood resilience measures to their homes.
* **Lack of Incentive:** Even when modifications are permitted, tenants have little financial incentive to invest in physical risk-reducing measures for a property they do not own. 
* **Short Tenancies:** Tenancies are often short, meaning residents have limited security of tenure and are typically less aware of the specific historical flood risks in their neighbourhoods.
* **Communication Barriers:** Private landlords can be particularly difficult for local authorities and agencies to engage with regarding residential flood protection planning. Furthermore, tenants in the private rented sector are subject to higher turnover rates, making it harder for official flood guidance and information to consistently reach them. 

**Data Source:** 
The data is derived from the 2011 UK Census (Office for National Statistics), specifically utilizing Census table KS402. It is calculated by taking the number of households classified as 'Rented: Private Landlord or Letting Agency' plus 'Rented: Other', dividing this sum by the total number of households, and multiplying by 100. The resulting spatial data is mapped down to the Lower Super Output Area (LSOA) level in England and Wales, Data Zones (DZ) in Scotland, and Super Output Areas (SOA) in Northern Ireland.
