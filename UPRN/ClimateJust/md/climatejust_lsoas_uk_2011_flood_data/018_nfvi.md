### Neighbourhood Flood Vulnerability Index (NFVI)

**Description:** 
This field represents the Neighbourhood Flood Vulnerability Index (NFVI), a composite measure providing insight into the social vulnerability of a neighbourhood should a flood occur. It estimates the propensity of individuals to experience a loss of well-being if exposed to a flood, as well as their capacity to prepare for, respond to, and recover from a flood event without significant emergency support from authorities.

**Vulnerability Rationale / Components:** 
The NFVI provides a comprehensive view of vulnerability by combining five key characteristics:
*   **Susceptibility:** The predisposition of an individual to experience harm, heavily influenced by age (the very young and the elderly) and existing health conditions.
*   **Ability to prepare:** The capacity to take actions during normal conditions to reduce future harm, which is influenced by income, property tenure, local knowledge, and information use.
*   **Ability to respond:** The underlying reasons why some act more effectively in the run-up to and during an event, driven by physical mobility, crime rates, income, and capacity to use information.
*   **Ability to recover:** The degree to which individuals can aid their own long-term recovery, affected heavily by income, mobility, and information use.
*   **Community support:** The availability of institutional and social help, gauged through housing characteristics, the community's collective experience of past floods, local social networks, and the flood exposure of local services like care homes, schools, and emergency responders.

**Data Source and Calculation:** 
The index is built from a broad hierarchy of data, starting with 27 individual supporting variables that feed into 12 composite indicators, which in turn form the five core characteristics. The final NFVI score is calculated by transforming the values of each indicator into *z-scores* and summing them with an equal weighting. The data is largely derived from the 2011 UK Census and the 2017 assessment by Sayers and Partners for the Joseph Rowntree Foundation, mapped to the Lower Super Output Area (LSOA) level in England and Wales, Data Zones (DZ) in Scotland, and Super Output Areas (SOA) in Northern Ireland.
