### Income deprivation index

**Description:** 
This field represents the percentage of people who are income deprived in a given area. It serves as a supporting variable for the "Income" indicator, which is used to assess a community's "Inability to Prepare," "Inability to Respond," and "Inability to Recover" characteristics within the Neighbourhood Flood Vulnerability Index (NFVI).

**Vulnerability Rationale:** 
A higher proportion of people on low incomes in an area indicates a higher level of social vulnerability to flooding. Low income restricts a household's ability to adapt to, prepare for, and recover from extreme weather events based on several key factors:
* **Inability to Prepare:** Low-income households are significantly less likely to have the financial capacity to prepare for future floods, such as purchasing insurance or implementing property-level protection measures. 
* **Housing Restrictions:** Low-income households are less likely to own their own homes, and this lack of ownership restricts their ability to make physical modifications to improve flood resilience.
* **Lack of Savings for Recovery:** A lack of savings restricts a household's ability to immediately respond to flood damage, such as paying for necessary repairs, replacing essential goods, or finding temporary accommodation to kick-start the recovery process.

**Data Source:** 
The data is sourced from the Office for National Statistics (ONS), National Records of Scotland, and the Northern Ireland Department for Communities, using a 2010 baseline. The specific indices used vary slightly by country:
* **England and Wales:** Derived from the Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD) Average Weekly Household Net Income Estimate (equivalised after housing costs).
* **Scotland:** Sourced from the Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation (SIMD) Income index, measuring the percentage of people who are income deprived.
* **Northern Ireland:** Sourced from the Northern Ireland Multiple Deprivation Measure (NIIMD) 2010.

The resulting spatial data is mapped down to the Lower Super Output Area (LSOA) level in England and Wales, Data Zones (DZ) in Scotland, and Super Output Areas (SOA) in Northern Ireland.
