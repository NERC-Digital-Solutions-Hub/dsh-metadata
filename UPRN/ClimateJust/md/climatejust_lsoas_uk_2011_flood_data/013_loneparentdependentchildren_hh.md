### % lone-parent households with dependent children

**Description:** 
This field represents the percentage of households in a given area that are headed by a lone parent with one or more dependent children. It serves as a supporting variable for the "Social networks" indicator, which is used to assess a community's "Community Support" as well as its "Ability to Respond" and "Ability to Recover" characteristics within the Neighbourhood Flood Vulnerability Index (NFVI).

**Vulnerability Rationale:** 
A higher proportion of lone parents with dependent children is used as a proxy indicator for higher social vulnerability, primarily due to the potential for social isolation and the burden of sole responsibility. The inclusion of this metric is based on the following factors:
* **Lack of Within-Family Support:** Lone parents face significant practical difficulties in preparing for and responding to a flood because they have sole responsibility for their children and lack direct, within-the-family support to share the physical and emotional burden.
* **Response Challenges:** Adults living without another adult in the household are more likely to struggle to take immediate action upon receiving a flood warning—such as moving furniture—and may feel more uncertain and anxious having no one to confide in during the crisis.
* **Important Caveat (Low Confidence):** The documentation notes that using this specific indicator as a proxy for social isolation has a "low" confidence level and is open to debate. There is conflicting evidence suggesting that while lone parents have greater responsibilities that limit their capacity to respond, they may sometimes possess *stronger* local community networks compared to other groups, which could offset some risks of isolation. 

**Data Source:** 
The data is derived from the 2011 UK Census (Office for National Statistics), specifically using Census table QS113. It is calculated by dividing the number of households containing a lone parent with one or more dependent children by the total number of households, and multiplying by 100. The resulting data is mapped down to the Lower Super Output Area (LSOA) level in England and Wales, Data Zones (DZ) in Scotland, and Super Output Areas (SOA) in Northern Ireland.
