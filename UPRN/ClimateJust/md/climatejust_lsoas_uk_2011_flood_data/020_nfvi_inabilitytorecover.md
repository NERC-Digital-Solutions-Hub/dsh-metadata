### Inability to Recover (Ability to Recover Characteristic)

**Description:** 
This field represents one of the five core characteristics of the Neighbourhood Flood Vulnerability Index (NFVI). It measures the personal, environmental, and social factors that influence the degree to which an individual or community can aid their own long-term recovery following a flood event. 

**Vulnerability Rationale / Components:** 
A higher score in this index indicates a higher *inability* (and thus greater social vulnerability) to bounce back and recover from the impacts of a flood. Many flood events have demonstrated the extended length of time it takes for communities to recover, which is largely influenced by three main domains in this 2011 flood dataset:
*   **Income:** Financial deprivation (measured via unemployment, long-term unemployment, low-income occupations, and general income deprivation) heavily restricts a household's ability to replace lost or damaged goods, find temporary accommodation, and pay for necessary repairs.
*   **Information Use:** Issues with language and familiarity (measured by the proportion of recent arrivals to the UK and those with low English proficiency) limit an individual's ability to understand what help is available and how to navigate the recovery process.
*   **Physical Mobility:** Restricted mobility (measured by high levels of disability, lack of private transport, and people living in medical or care establishments) creates practical and physical barriers to independently managing post-flood recovery logistics.

**Data Source and Calculation:** 
The characteristic index is calculated by transforming the underlying supporting variables for the three domains into *z-scores* and summing them assuming an equal weighting. The foundational data is largely derived from the 2011 UK Census (Office for National Statistics) and the 2017 assessment by Sayers and Partners. The final data is mapped at the neighbourhood level, corresponding to Lower Super Output Areas (LSOAs) in England and Wales, Data Zones (DZs) in Scotland, and Super Output Areas (SOAs) in Northern Ireland.
