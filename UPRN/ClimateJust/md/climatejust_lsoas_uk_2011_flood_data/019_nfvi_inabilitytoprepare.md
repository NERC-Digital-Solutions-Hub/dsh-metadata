### Inability to Prepare (Ability to Prepare Characteristic)

**Description:** 
This field is one of the five core characteristics that make up the Neighbourhood Flood Vulnerability Index (NFVI). It measures the personal and social factors that affect an individual's or community's capacity to take actions during normal conditions—meaning in the absence of a forecast or an actual flood event—that are likely to reduce the harm they suffer when a future flood occurs. 

**Vulnerability Rationale / Components:** 
A community's ability to prepare for an extreme weather event is governed primarily by social factors. A higher score in this index indicates a higher *inability* (and thus greater vulnerability) to take precautionary measures, such as purchasing insurance or installing property-level protection. The index is constructed by combining indicators across four main domains:
*   **Income:** Financial deprivation (measured through unemployment, long-term unemployment, low-income occupations, and income deprivation) severely restricts a household's ability to afford technical solutions, flood preparations, and insurance. 
*   **Property Tenure:** Renters (both private and social) are highly vulnerable because they generally have far less freedom and ability to make physical flood-resilience modifications to their living environments compared to home owners.
*   **Information Use:** Populations with a higher proportion of recent arrivals to the UK or people with lower English proficiency face language and cultural barriers that limit their ability to access, understand, and use official information and guidance.
*   **Local Knowledge:** A higher proportion of new migrants to a local area indicates higher vulnerability, as these individuals lack the personal, family, and community experience of past local flood events and may be unaware of the specific risks their new community faces.

**Data Source and Calculation:** 
The characteristic index is calculated by transforming the underlying variables for the domains mentioned above into *z-scores* and combining them using an equal weighting. The foundational data is largely derived from the 2011 UK Census (Office for National Statistics) and is mapped at the neighbourhood level, corresponding to Lower Super Output Areas (LSOAs) in England and Wales, Data Zones (DZ) in Scotland, and Super Output Areas (SOA) in Northern Ireland.
