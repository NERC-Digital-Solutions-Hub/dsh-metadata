### Social renters (% Households renting from Social or Council landlords)

**Description:** 
This field represents the percentage of households in a given area that rent their accommodation from a social landlord or the local council. It serves as a supporting variable (often referenced as T2) for the "Property tenure" indicator, which is used to assess a community's "Ability to Prepare" characteristic within the Neighbourhood Flood Vulnerability Index (NFVI).

**Vulnerability Rationale:** 
A higher proportion of people renting their homes from social landlords indicates a higher level of social vulnerability to flooding. Similar to private renters, social tenants are generally more vulnerable because they have less capacity and autonomy to adapt their homes compared to homeowners. The rationale for this includes:
* **Restrictions on Modifications:** Tenants are often not permitted to make physical alterations to their properties to install permanent flood resilience measures.
* **Compounding Vulnerabilities:** Social housing tenants may encounter additional difficulties in preparing for and responding to flooding because they are more likely to have a lower income, meaning they cannot easily afford meaningful physical risk-reducing measures.
* **Lack of Incentive and Awareness:** Tenancies can be shorter with limited security of tenure, meaning residents are often less aware of the historical flood risks in their specific neighbourhoods. 
* ***Important Caveat*:** While being a tenant increases vulnerability, it is noted that social renters may uniquely benefit from wide-scale adaptations and support systems put in place by their social landlords or councils, which private renters generally lack.

**Data Source:** 
The data is derived from the 2011 UK Census (Office for National Statistics), specifically utilizing Census tables such as KS402. It is calculated by taking the number of households classified as 'Rented: Council (Local authority)' plus 'Rented: Other social rented', dividing this sum by the total number of households, and multiplying by 100. The resulting spatial data is mapped down to the Lower Super Output Area (LSOA) level in England and Wales, Data Zones (DZ) in Scotland, and Super Output Areas (SOA) in Northern Ireland.
