### Low income occupations (% in routine or semi-routine occupations)

**Description:** 
This field represents the percentage of people aged 16 to 74 in a given area who are employed in routine or semi-routine occupations. It serves as a supporting variable for the "Income" indicator, which is used to assess a community's "Inability to Prepare," "Inability to Respond," and "Inability to Recover" characteristics within the Neighbourhood Flood Vulnerability Index (NFVI).

**Vulnerability Rationale:** 
A higher proportion of people employed in routine and semi-routine jobs indicates a higher level of social vulnerability to flooding. This metric is primarily used as a strong proxy for low income and financial deprivation based on several factors:
* **Severe Wealth Inequality:** All other things being equal, people working in routine or semi-routine occupations tend to be on lower incomes. Evidence suggests that average wealth levels for households headed by someone in a routine occupation are only 14% of the wealth held by those in 'large employer or higher managerial' roles. 
* **Inability to Adapt:** This stark lack of financial resources significantly restricts a household's capacity to afford the necessary ways to prepare for, respond to, and recover from extreme weather events.
* **Compounding Vulnerabilities:** People in these occupations may also face additional forms of vulnerability, such as higher exposure to hazards directly tied to the physical characteristics of their jobs, or increased susceptibility to harm because they are statistically more likely to experience ill-health.

**Data Source:** 
The data is derived from the 2011 UK Census (Office for National Statistics), specifically utilizing Census table KS611. It is calculated by taking the number of people aged 16 to 74 in routine occupations plus the number in semi-routine occupations, dividing this sum by all people aged 16 to 74, and multiplying by 100. The resulting spatial data is mapped down to the Lower Super Output Area (LSOA) level in England and Wales, Data Zones (DZ) in Scotland, and Super Output Areas (SOA) in Northern Ireland.
