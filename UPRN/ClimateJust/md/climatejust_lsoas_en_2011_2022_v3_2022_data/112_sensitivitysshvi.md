### Sensitivity Index

**Description:**
This field represents the standardized score (Z-score) for the **Sensitivity** dimension of socio-spatial vulnerability within a given Lower Super Output Area (LSOA). Sensitivity refers to the personal biophysical characteristics of a population that affect the likelihood that an extreme climate event—such as a heatwave or a flood—will have negative health and welfare impacts. For example, the elderly and very young children tend to be much more physically susceptible to the harmful effects of extreme weather.

**Vulnerability Context:**
The Sensitivity Index is a composite metric calculated as an equally weighted combination of neighbourhood-level scores from two primary domains:
1.  **Age:** Indicators include the percentage of young children (under 5 years old) and older people (over 75 years old) within the population.
2.  **Health:** In the 2022 England dataset, health indicators include rates of emergency hospital admissions, comparative illness and disability ratios, mood and anxiety disorder indicators, medical and care residents, years of potential life lost, and homelessness rates.

**Interpretation:**
Because the index is presented as a standardized Z-score, it evaluates how a specific neighbourhood's biophysical sensitivity compares to the overall average across England:
*   **Positive values (High scores):** Denote a higher concentration of sensitive populations compared to the English average, indicating greater vulnerability. Scores ≥ 2.5 are classified as "Acute", scores between 1.5 and 2.5 are "Extremely high", and scores between 0.5 and 1.5 are "Relatively high".
*   **Values around 0 (-0.5 to 0.5):** Denote that the area's sensitivity is approximately equal to the national average.
*   **Negative values (Low scores):** Denote lower sensitivity compared to the English average, scaling down through "Relatively low" (-0.5 to -1.5), "Extremely low" (-1.5 to -2.5), and "Slight" (≤ -2.5).
