### Inability to Recover Index

**Description:**
This field represents the "(In)ability to Recover" dimension of the Socio-Spatial Climate Vulnerability Index. It evaluates the underlying personal, social, and environmental factors that govern a person's or community's capacity to bounce back and recover after experiencing a climate or extreme weather event, such as a flood or heatwave. 

**Domains:**
Depending on whether it is measuring vulnerability to floods or heat, the index is calculated as an equally-weighted combination of neighbourhood-level scores across several key domains that dictate recovery capabilities:
*   **Income:** Measures financial deprivation, including the proportion of employment-deprived and income-deprived people. A lack of financial resources restricts the ability to replace lost goods, pay for repairs, or find temporary accommodation.
*   **Language:** Evaluates the level of English proficiency within the population, which affects an individual's ability to understand what post-event help is available and how to access it.
*   **Internet:** Assesses digital connectivity (e.g., proportion of premises with sub-standard or lacking superfast broadband), which impacts access to online recovery resources and support networks.
*   **Social Networks:** Evaluates the availability of personal and community support networks (often indicated by the proportion of single-pensioner households or lack of school-related networks). Socially isolated individuals are less likely to obtain assistance from friends or family during the recovery phase.
*   **Mobility:** Assesses general physical mobility and disability, as well as access to private transport.
*   **Service Access:** Specifically prominent in heat vulnerability, this evaluates the availability and accessibility of essential health services, including travel times to GPs, hospitals, and pharmacies, which are critical if someone requires medical attention following an event.
*   **House Prices / Housing Mobility:** Used in the flood vulnerability context, this evaluates indicators such as the median price paid for residential properties, which acts as a proxy for whether residents are trapped in an area or have the financial housing mobility to move away.

**Interpretation:**
The index is represented as a Z-score to measure relative vulnerability compared to the national average. The scores are classified into categories ranging from **Slight** (≤ -2.5) to **Acute** (≥ 2.5). Higher positive scores identify neighbourhoods that face significant social and resource barriers to recovering from extreme weather impacts, indicating communities that may experience more severe or prolonged disruptions to their well-being.
