# Property Tenure

**Reference:** T1 and T2

**Theme:** Vulnerability

**Hazard reference:** Flood (Revised Data, 2017)

**Characteristic:** Ability to Prepare

**Indicator:** Property Tenure

**Supporting Variable:** Private Renters and Social Renters (see Data Sources below)

**Assumption:**

More information about people renting their homes and social vulnerability, as well as what can be done to help, is available at: [http://www.climatejust.org.uk/messages/tenants-0](http://www.climatejust.org.uk/messages/tenants-0)

Social housing tenants may encounter difficulties in preparing for and responding to flooding due to their living arrangements and because they are likely to have a low income.

Tenants are often not allowed to make physical alterations to their properties, and leaseholders may be disinclined to do so as they may not feel the additional expense is worthwhile given that they do not own the freehold. Landlords of social housing may be more inclined to make these alterations, but little quantified evidence exists.

Where tenants are permitted to make physical alterations to their dwellings, there is little incentive to do so. This may be because:

- Tenancies are often short, with limited security of tenure, so residents are likely to be less aware of flood risk in their neighbourhoods.
- Tenants are generally less well-off than homeowners (The Poverty Site, 2014) and therefore cannot afford to install meaningful physical risk-reducing measures.
- Tenants are less likely than homeowners to speak English as their first language and so may not be easily able to access information on flood risk and preparedness.
