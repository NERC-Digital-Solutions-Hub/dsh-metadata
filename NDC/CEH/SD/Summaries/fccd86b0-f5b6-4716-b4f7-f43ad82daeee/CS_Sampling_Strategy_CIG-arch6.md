# The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey (up to 2007)
Revised and Updated from: 'The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey', C.J. Barr, September 1998. DETR CONTRACT No. CR0212  
Revised by C.M.Wood CEH Lancaster, Library Avenue, Bailrigg, Lancaster. LA1 4AP

## Overview and aim
- Documents the evolution of the Countryside Survey sampling framework up to 2007.
- Describes how the ITE Land Classification is used to stratify sampling and derive national, regional, and country-specific habitat estimates.
- Emphasises data products (habitat areas, land-class maps, field-collection data) that enable policy-relevant analyses and public exploration.

## Core concepts and approach
- Use of stratified random sampling based on the ITE Land Classification to ensure representative coverage of Britain’s varied landscapes.
- Sampling units are 1 km squares; sampling frames are built from centre squares of a 15 × 15 km grid, with surrounding squares classified to extend context.
- Early methodology relied on objective stratification and, for sampling decisions, simple multivariate classification; the approach evolves with increasing computing power and data needs.
- Data products include national, regional, and country-specific estimates of habitat areas, derived by combining class proportions with class areas; outputs include maps and descriptive reports.

## Evolution of the ITE Land Classification and sampling framework
- 1978–CS1978: First Land Classification using Indicator Species Analysis (ISA); 32 classes derived from environmental attributes; 1228 centre squares classified; eight squares per Land Class surveyed (total 256 squares); national estimates based on mean habitat area per square within each class.
- 1984–CS1984: Second field survey; sample expanded to 12 km squares per class (384 total); national estimates still anchored to the 1978 Land Classification.
- 1990–CS1990: All GB squares classified into an updated Land Classification; 508 squares surveyed; inclusion of the first Land Cover Map; repeat vegetation quadrats from 1978; national estimates recalculated using the revised classification; urban/sea corrections implemented.
- 1990s–land classification refinements: Attempts to classify every GB square with a reduced data load; 62% correspondence between old and new classifications for squares, with most misclassifications remaining in neighboring classes; implications for regional and national estimates acknowledged.
- 2000–CS2000: Emphasis on separate country estimates (England & Wales vs Scotland); subdivision of Land Classes into country-unit versions; aggregation of underrepresented classes to maintain adequate representation; additional squares allocated to ensure coverage of new classes; inclusion of upland habitat sampling module to improve upland estimates.
- 2007–CS2007: Wales-focused reporting drives amendments:
  - Sub-division of Land Classes into five Wales/England country-unit classes (5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w) to enable Wales-only estimates.
  - Creation of 40 strata (from 40 in CS2000 to 45 in CS2007 due to sub-divisions).
  - Extra sampling in Wales (43 additional squares; minimum of 6 squares per Welsh class); two English classes reduced to four squares per class, with limited impact on English estimate precision.
  - Wales gains more detailed country-specific outputs; England adjustments implemented to compensate for Welsh increases.
- Isle of Man: earlier samples replaced in country-unit framework; two IOM squares no longer contribute to country estimates.

## Sampling details and outputs by year
- 1978: 32 Land Classes; 1228 centre squares classified; 6039 km squares represented; 256 total sample squares (8 per class); national estimates via mean habitat area per square within each class multiplied by class area.
- 1984: 384 squares (12 per class); continued use of 1978 Land Classification for national estimates.
- 1990: 508 squares; Land Classification revised to cover all GB squares; urban/sea corrections; results published in CS1990 main report.
- 2000 (CS2000): 40 strata; country-unit framework introduced; 19 additional squares to ensure representation of new classes; upland habitat module added.
- 2007 (CS2007): 45 strata (5 Wales sub-classes); 43 Welsh extra squares; targeted Wales-only reporting; some English classes reduced to maintain balance.

## Country coverage, uplands, and data consistency
- England & Wales vs Scotland country-unit estimates established; Isle of Man sampling replaced to align with country reporting.
- Upland habitats module: extra 30 squares in upland/marginal upland areas in England & Wales to improve statistical precision for upland estimates.
- Data consistency: changing land classifications affects cross-survey comparability; preference is to use the latest classification within a survey, with cross-survey consistency considerations noted (Table 4 contrasts original vs. latest classifications).

## Data products, interpretation, and references
- Outputs include land-class maps, sample-square distributions, and national/regional habitat estimates; results underpin ecological mapping, landscape change assessment, and policy-relevant reporting.
- The document situates these methodological choices within a long history of UK countryside survey work and provides references for deeper technical detail.
