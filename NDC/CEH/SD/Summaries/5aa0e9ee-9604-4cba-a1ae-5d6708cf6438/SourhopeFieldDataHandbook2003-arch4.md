## Measurements and variables captured

- Soil profile characteristics
  - Depths and horizon delineations; color and mottling; texture (e.g., sandy silt loam, clay loam); structure (blocky, granular, platy); root presence; boundary clarity.
- Soil chemical and physical properties
  - Nitrogen and carbon content (%N, %C), carbon-to-nitrogen ratios, cation contents (Ca, Mg, K, Na), moisture-related metrics, LOI, pH in water and CaCl2.
- Auger sample analyses
  - Same chemical suite as horizon samples, aligned to specific auger depths; supports finer-scale comparisons within plots.
- Vegetation data
  - Species inventories, plot-level totals, and subplot-level occurrences, enabling vegetation classifications (e.g., NVC-associated grassland types) and indicator-species analyses.
- Data provenance
  - Each sample tied to a barcode, horizon depth, and plot/block, ensuring traceability and reproducibility.

## Data quality, coverage, and challenges

- Coverage and sampling
  - Variable sampling across blocks and horizons; some subplots have missing data or irregular coverage, requiring cautious interpretation for site-wide summaries.
- Anomalies and heterogeneity
  - Notable block-to-block variance in soil parameters; some horizons show unusual readings (e.g., anomalous moisture loss, LOI, pH), underscoring spatial heterogeneity and the need for robust spatial-statistical handling.
- Metadata and standardization needs
  - Consistent horizon naming, depth intervals, and unit conventions are essential for cross-block integration and long-term comparability.
- Data integration
  - Integrating soil and vegetation datasets across blocks, plots, and subplots poses challenges for meta-analysis and cross-site synthesis, despite a strong multi-layer design.

## Implications and opportunities for Data Leaders

- Longitudinal value
  - Rich, multi-scale, time-series data enabling analysis of soil-vegetation dynamics, upland grassland responses, and biodiversity patterns over time.
- Cross-site synthesis potential
  - Alignment with ECN and SERAD networks enables cross-site comparisons and policy-relevant insights on soil-plant interactions.
- Data strategy opportunities
  - Develop centralized, machine-actionable data repositories with standardized metadata schemas, horizon nomenclature, and depth intervals.
  - Implement consistent data governance: metadata templates, data quality checks, and clear documentation of sampling protocols.
  - Facilitate data portals for researchers and policymakers to access block- and plot-level soil and vegetation datasets, with provenance and versioning.
- Practical recommendations
  - Harmonize horizon naming conventions and unit reporting; document sampling densities and coverage per block.
  - Integrate soil and vegetation datasets through a common data model to support meta-analyses and cross-block syntheses.
  - Maintain robust archival practices (e.g., horizon-level archives, sample storage metadata) to ensure future comparability and re-analysis.
