# The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey

- Purpose and scope
  - Outlines the field survey sampling strategy for Countryside Survey (CS), up to 2007.
  - Shows how the ITE Land Classification has evolved and how sampling has been linked to national environmental monitoring and policy needs.
  - Emphasises the importance of representativeness, comparability over time, and linkage to landscape/environmental classifications.

- Core concepts: ITE Land Classification and stratified sampling
  - Land Classification serves as the stratification framework to ensure samples cover the range of GB environments.
  - Sampling unit: 1 km squares representing broader landscape classes.
  - Stratification approach aims to reduce bias and capture ecological variability within discrete strata (land classes).
  - Early method used a centred approach: centre squares of a 15 x 15 km grid across GB, plus four surrounding squares for each classified centre.

- Evolution of the sampling framework by survey round
  - CS1978 (Initial Land Classification and 1st Field Survey)
    - ISA-based classification produced 32 land classes from environmental data for 1228 centre squares.
    - Sampled eight 1 km squares per Land Class (256 total) for field survey.
    - National habitat estimates derived from mean habitat area per square within a class multiplied by the class area.
  - CS1984 (2nd Field Survey)
    - Increased sample to 12 squares per land class (384 total), adding to existing squares.
  - CS1990 ('All Squares' Land Classification, 3rd Field Survey)
    - Land Classification revised to classify every 1 km square in GB.
    - 508 squares surveyed; retained features from 1984 and re-collected vegetation data.
    - Introduced linkage with the first Land Cover Map (from satellite data).
  - Developments post-1990: extending classification to GB-wide coverage
    - With greater computing power, the GB-wide classification was extended using climatic, geological and physiographic surrogates.
    - Original 1228-class structure compared with new schemes showed 62% class correspondence; non-matching squares generally fell into neighboring classes, preserving overall class characteristics.
    - Result: need to re-balance sampling across a more comprehensive, but not perfectly aligned, GB-wide set of land classes.
  - CS2000 (4th Field Survey)
    - Addressed policy-driven needs: separate country estimates (England & Wales vs Scotland) due to devolution and regional policy needs.
    - Land Classes subdivided into country-unit versions; aggregated where necessary to maintain representation; 40 strata.
    - Additional squares added to ensure adequate representation of new/modified classes in each country; upland habitat sampling module introduced to improve accuracy for uplands.
    - Wales remained heavily sampled relative to its area; dedicated adjustments to England for maintaining balance with loss of Welsh squares.
  - CS2007 (5th Field Survey)
    - Wales-only reporting requirements led to further Wales-specific class subdivisions (5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w) and an expanded 45 strata.
    - Added 43 Welsh squares to ensure each Welsh class had at least 6 squares; England classes adjusted due to Welsh expansion and to preserve overall sampling consistency.
    - Wales became the most intensively sampled country unit; England saw relatively small reductions in some classes’ sample counts.
    - Upland module retained/increased to improve country-specific upland statistics.

- National estimates, country reporting, and comparability
  - National estimates can be calculated using different land classifications (original 1990 vs latest) leading to potential inconsistencies; a 2006 scoping note emphasizes choosing a framework that balances consistency with cross-survey comparability.
  - For Wales and Scotland, country-unit versions of land classes are used to produce country-specific estimates; CS2000 and CS2007 introduced country-focused sampling and reporting.
  - Tables and figures in the document illustrate sample counts, strata areas, and sampling rates across CS1978, CS1984, CS1990, CS2000, and CS2007.

- Upland module and additional survey components
  - CS2000 introduced an upland/marginal upland survey module with 30 extra squares in upland classes to improve statistical accuracy for upland habitats in England and Wales.
  - This module enhanced country-specific habitat estimates where upland data previously suffered from under-sampling.

- Data usage and outputs
  - Outputs include habitat area estimates and change statistics across land classes and countries, informing national and regional environmental policy and monitoring.
  - The strategy supports time-series analyses by maintaining a stratified sampling framework tied to consistent land classifications, while accommodating policy-driven reporting needs.

- Key takeaways for monitoring framework authors
  - Stratified, random sampling using an evolving land/classification framework helps ensure representative coverage and robust time-series analyses.
  - As classifications evolve (e.g., country-unit subdivisions), sampling plans must adapt to maintain country-specific estimates without compromising comparability.
  - Balancing representation across a growing number of land classes requires adding targeted samples in underrepresented classes and/or countries.
  - Upland and marginal-habitat modules can significantly improve the precision of estimates for environmentally sensitive areas.
  - Clear documentation of classification changes and sampling adjustments is essential to interpret national estimates consistently over time.
