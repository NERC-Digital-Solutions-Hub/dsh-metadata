# The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey (up to 2007)

- Overview
  - Documents the development and application of the ITE Land Classification as the sampling framework for Countryside Survey (CS) field surveys from 1978 through 2007.
  - Emphasizes the importance of stratified, representative sampling and the evolution of methods to support national and country-specific environmental policy reporting.

- The ITE Land Classification: foundation for stratified sampling
  - Built to stratify land into discrete classes based on environmental attributes (altitude, geology, climate, etc.).
  - Allows sampling to be drawn from well-defined strata to minimise bias and ensure representative coverage of ecological variation.
  - Original approach used 1 km squares as the sampling unit; environmental data collected for classification guided the stratification.

- Evolution of the sampling framework by survey year
  - 1978 CS1978 – Initial Land Classification and Field Survey
    - Indicator Species Analysis (ISA) used to group 1 km squares into 32 land classes from 1228 centre squares (14 x 15 km grid).
    - 8 samples drawn per class, total of 256 squares surveyed in summer 1977–1978.
    - National habitat area estimates derived as mean area per square in each land class times the land-class area.
  - 1984 CS1984 – Second Field Survey
    - Sampling expanded to 12 squares per land class (total 384) to improve estimates.
    - Focus shifted toward land use and habitat mapping; results published later.
  - 1990 CS1990 – All Squares Land Classification & Third Field Survey
    - Land Classification revised to classify every square in GB (all 1 km squares), with some squares recoded.
    - 508 squares surveyed; repeat of 1978 vegetation quadrats; introduction of first Land Cover Map (satellite-derived).
    - National estimates still produced using the 1990 Land Classification framework.
  - 2000 CS2000 – Revised Land Classification & Separate Country Estimates
    - Classification updated to better support regional and local estimates; major change to enable separate England with Wales and Scotland reporting.
    - Land Classes subdivided into country-unit versions (creating 40 strata); additional squares added to ensure adequate representation (minimum 6 squares per new class in each country where possible).
    - An uplands module added, with 30 extra squares placed in upland classes to improve accuracy for England & Wales.
    - Wales became a focal point for more intensive sampling to support Wales-only reporting.
  - 2007 CS2007 – Welsh-only Reporting & Further Welsh Adjustments
    - Wales-only reporting requirements driven by devolved governance led to further Wales-focused adjustments.
    - Land Classes subdivided into England-Wales country-unit variants, increasing strata to 45.
    - Added 43 Welsh squares to ensure adequate representation; some English classes reduced to 4 squares in specific cases.
    - England adjustments justified as having relatively small loss of precision; Wales maintained robust representation.
    - Revised maps and tables reflect England, Wales, and Scotland sampling distributions.

- How estimates were derived and presented
  - National habitat estimates produced by combining per-square results within each land class with the land-class area across GB.
  - When classifications were updated (e.g., CS1990, CS2000, CS2007), national estimates could be generated using the latest classification or the original 1990 classification, with the 2006 scoping study recommending the latter for cross-survey consistency.
  - Tables and figures (e.g., Tables 2 and 3, Figures 4–7) illustrate the distribution of squares, class subdivisions, and country-specific sampling frameworks.

- Practical considerations and notes
  - The sampling strategy consistently aimed to balance statistical robustness with policy-relevant reporting needs (England, Scotland, Wales; uplands; devolved reporting requirements).
  - The approach acknowledges trade-offs between class representation, survey effort, and the need for comparable national estimates across surveys.
  - The document includes detailed acknowledgements, references, and methodological notes on the evolution of the Land Classification and its application to Countryside Survey.

- End notes
  - Note Regarding the Creation of National Estimates: Changing the basic classification used for national estimates affects consistency of stock estimates; the preferred approach for a given survey is to use the latest classification for internal consistency, while the original classification provides cross-survey comparability.
