## Key data quality and validation points

- LCM2000 statistics are described as uncalibrated and are validated against FS field surveys (CS2000) with 95% confidence intervals.
- Differences between LCM2000 and FS are acknowledged and explained in accompanying text; coastal coverage varies due to tidal states and inclusion/exclusion of offshore inter-tidal areas.
- Some entries are marked n/a, reflecting gaps in data availability or survey coverage by country.
- The matrices provide a structured crosswalk between classifications, aiding data integration but also revealing potential misalignments between schemes.

## Implications for Data Leaders

- Demonstrates the importance of harmonising land-cover classifications across partners to enable coherent data strategy and cross-network analytics.
- Shows the value of a robust crosswalk between classification schemes (LCM2000, CS2000) for policy analysis, planning, and monitoring across the UK.
- Highlights data management needs: documenting definitions, variants, grid resolution (25 m), update cycles, and metadata to support discoverability and re-use.
- Underlines data gaps and access barriers (gaps in NI data for certain measures, n/a entries, coastal intertidal considerations) that impede a full-system view.

## Practical actions and recommendations for Data Leaders

- Standardise habitat classifications and maintain a clear, versioned crosswalk between LCM2000, CS2000, and other partner schemes to support interoperability.
- Invest in metadata and provenance: document target classes, subclasses, variants, grid resolution, data sources, calibration status, and update schedules.
- Enhance data discovery and access: ensure datasets are findable, with accessible metadata, and address barriers such as paywalls or restricted data sources.
- Prioritise calibration efforts: use FS field survey results to iteratively calibrate LCM2000-derived estimates, focusing on habitats with large discrepancies or high policy relevance.
- Develop data-collection plans to fill gaps (e.g., NI coverage gaps, coastal/offshore areas) and to reduce reliance on a single data source.
- Promote co-ownership of data products by engaging policy colleagues and external partners in validating classifications and outputs, iterating based on user needs.
- Use the correspondence matrices to inform data integration in dashboards and reporting tools, while clearly communicating uncertainty ranges (confidence intervals).
