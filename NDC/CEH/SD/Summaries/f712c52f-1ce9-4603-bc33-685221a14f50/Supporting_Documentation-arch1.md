# Supporting documentation

- Part of the Dynamic Drivers of Disease in Africa Consortium (DDDAC) research; data contributed to consortium work.
- Funded by NERC project NE/J000701/1 with support from the Ecosystem Services for Poverty Alleviation Programme (ESPA).
- In Zimbabwe, an interdisciplinary team (sociologists, GIS experts, entomologists) conducted the study; sociologists gathered qualitative data and others contributed methods and analysis.
- Dataset purpose: information about how local people in Mukwichi communal lands of Hurungwe (Chundu Ward) are affected by and respond to tsetse flies and trypanosomiasis.
- Data collection period: interviews conducted December 2012 to August 2013; key informants selected from local community members.

- Consent and ethics:
  - Verbal consent obtained from all participants prior to interviews.
  - Interviews semi-structured with a flexible topic guide; aim to cover all relevant issues with each informant.
  - Anonymisation measures implemented to protect identities.

- Interview design and topics:
  - Provisional research questions developed by University of Zimbabwe researchers, with local research assistant support.
  - Interview topics covered: history of settlement, livestock introduction/ownership, migrants and cattle, tsetse-related household problems (agriculture, labor, health), problem areas and tsetse patches, strategies for dealing with tsetse, sleeping sickness, and Nagana, effectiveness of modern medicines, traditional approaches, extent of damage and risks.

- Sampling and informants:
  - Respondents chosen for knowledge of disease and accessibility.
  - Aimed to capture a broad picture across three community clusters: (i) long-established Korekore villages; (ii) border villages with abundant tsetse populations and migrant land-seekers; (iii) transitional villages near frontier areas.

- Data handling and storage:
  - Interviews were recorded; transcripts created afterward.
  - Transcripts stored securely at the University of Zimbabwe.
  - To meet Environmental Information Data Centre requirements, transcripts were anonymised and converted from Word to Rich Text Format (RTF).

- Anonymisation and coding:
  - Individual names replaced with non-identifying placeholders; exact dates generalized to less precise dates when necessary.
  - Each interview coded with a letter and number to protect intervieweesâ€™ identities.
