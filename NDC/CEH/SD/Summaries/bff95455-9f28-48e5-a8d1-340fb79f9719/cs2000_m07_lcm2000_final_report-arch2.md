# Footnotes and methodological context (key points)

- LCM2000 statistics come from full-count cover on a 25 m grid; FS statistics are from Haines-Young et al. 2000.
- Differences between LCM2000 and FS reflect definitional and methodological variations; coastal coverage is variable due to tidal states and inclusion/exclusion of offshore inter-tidal areas.
- FS population coverage (1 km squares) differs by jurisdiction (e.g., NI exclusions).
- Tables frequently note data gaps (n/a) and use bootstrapping to estimate confidence intervals.
- The CS2000-to-LCM2000 mapping is approximate; several mappings are non-exclusive or require generalisation (urban, Broad vs Narrow classes).

# Implications for Analysts Monitoring the Environment

- Value proposition:
  - Provides a consistent, standardized framework to assess environmental health and policy performance over time.
  - Enables cross-country comparisons and temporal trend analysis using a common classification scheme.
- Data workflow:
  - Emphasizes data verification, quality assurance, cleaning, transformation, and storage in appropriate portals.
  - Supports integration of LCM2000/CS2000 with field survey data to improve interpretation.
- Challenges highlighted:
  - Increasing the value of datasets by combining them with other relevant data sources (e.g., CS2000) to reduce single-use limitations.
  - Facilitating access to underlying data used to create final outputs to support transparency and reproducibility.
  - Managing uncertainties and calibration needs due to uncalibrated LCM2000 data and imperfect correspondences with CS2000/FS classifications.
- Takeaway for practice:
  - Use standardized habitat mappings to track environmental health over time, but be mindful of calibration gaps and correspondence uncertainties when comparing with field surveys or alternative classifications.
