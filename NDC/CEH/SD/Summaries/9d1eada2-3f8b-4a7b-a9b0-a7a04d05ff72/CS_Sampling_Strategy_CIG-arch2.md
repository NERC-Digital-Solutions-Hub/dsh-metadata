# The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey (up to 2007) Revised and Updated from: 'The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey', C.J. Barr, September 1998. DETR CONTRACT No. CR0212

## Executive snapshot
- Describes the evolution of the ITE Land Classification and the sampling framework for Countryside Survey GB field surveys from 1978 to 2007.
- Emphasizes a stratified, random sampling design using a standardized land-class framework to ensure representative national, regional, and country-level estimates of habitats and land use.
- Details how classifications have expanded (32 → 40 → 45 classes) and how country-specific reporting and upland habitats were integrated.
- Outlines changes in survey design across CS1978, CS1984, CS1990, CS2000, and CS2007 to improve accuracy, comparability, and policy-relevant outputs.

## The ITE Land Classification: origins and purpose
- Stratified ecological sampling: classify land into discrete, meaningful classes to ensure representative samples across Great Britain.
- Early method: use environmental attributes (altitude, geology, climate, etc.) to form 1 km square classes via multivariate analysis (Indicator Species Analysis, ISA).
- Initial implementation created 32 land classes from a subset of GB squares; subsequent surveys expanded or revised classes to reflect broader data and policy needs.
- The Land Classification serves as the sampling framework for field surveys and as the basis for estimating national habitat areas.

## The Countryside Survey sampling framework – historical progression
- 1978 CS: 1 km classified squares identified; 8 sample squares per class (total 256) surveyed; focus on vegetation quadrats, soils, and habitat mapping.
- 1984 CS: sample size increased by 50% (12 squares per class; total 384) to improve precision; continued use of the original land-class framework.
- 1990 CS (CS1990): “All Squares” Land Classification applied; 508 squares surveyed; added Land Cover Mapping via satellite linkage; refinements allowed some squares to change class; national estimates recalculated with updated classifications.
- 1998 refinements: Land Classification expanded to 40 classes to support Scotland-only reporting; national estimates updated accordingly.

## Developments for Countryside Survey 2000 (CS2000)
- Need for country-specific estimates (England & Wales vs Scotland) prompted major framework changes.
- Introduced country-unit versions of Land Classes (sub-division) and class aggregation where needed, creating 40 strata.
- Added 19 extra squares to ensure adequate representation of new classes; additional adjustments to maintain balanced sampling between England and Wales (plus minor adjustments for Isle of Man).
- Wales-specific sampling emphasized due to devolved policy needs; introduced Welsh-only reporting capabilities.

## Wales and uplands: CS2007 adjustments
- Wales required Wales-only habitat status/change information; existing Wales sampling (64 squares) was deemed insufficient for robust Wales-only estimates.
- CS2007 introduced five new Welsh sub-classes (5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w) and expanded the number of strata to 45.
- Wales received additional samples to ensure minimum representation across new Welsh classes; England did not receive comparable increases, though precision loss in two English classes was assessed as acceptable.
- An uplands module (CS2000 onward) added 30 squares placed within upland land classes to improve habitat estimates for uplands in England and Wales.

## How national estimates are produced and consistency considerations
- National estimates are computed by combining class-level areas with square-level data, and by using the Land Classification as the standard frame across surveys.
- A key issue: changing the underlying land classification can affect the consistency of national stock estimates; thus, CS2000/CS2007 favored using the latest country-specific classifications for internal consistency, while recognizing cross-survey comparability challenges.

## Outputs, maps, and data stewardship
- Outputs include estimates of habitat areas by land class, trend analyses, and geographic representations (maps and tables).
- The sampling framework is designed to be transparent and reproducible, with explicit class definitions, sample allocations, and country-specific reporting pathways.
- The strategy underscores the importance of storing and integrating resulting datasets into appropriate data portals to enable broader access and secondary analyses.

## Practical implications for environmental analysts
- Provides a robust, long-term, standardized framework for monitoring habitat change across Great Britain, supporting policy evaluation and environmental health assessments.
- Ensures representativeness through stratified sampling and controlled expansion of land classes to align with devolved governance and upland habitat reporting.
- Highlights the trade-offs between historical continuity and the need to adopt current classifications for policy relevance; emphasizes documenting methodological changes to preserve interpretability of time-series data.
- Encourages dataset value enhancement by aligning with broader data sources (as per general monitoring challenges) and promoting data accessibility for broader reuse.
