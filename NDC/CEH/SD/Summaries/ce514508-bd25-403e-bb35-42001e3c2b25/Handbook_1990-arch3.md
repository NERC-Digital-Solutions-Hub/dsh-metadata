# COUNTRYSIDE SURVEY 1990

## Aims, scope and approach

- Purpose: identify and apply environmental health measures to scrutinise policy, support management decisions, and inform future actions.
- Design philosophy: a two-tier approach linking bottom-up field data (habitat, vegetation, land cover) with top-down satellite/remote-sensing information; emphasis on current datasets for scientific evaluation and trend detection.
- International and historical context: builds on ITE surveys (1977/8, 1984, 1988) and uses a consistent Land Classification System to enable comparability over time.
- Output focus: production of clear, openly shareable outputs (maps, tables, reports, dashboards) and establishment of data governance practices to ensure data quality, provenance and up-to-date metadata.

## Study design and sampling

- Scope: 512 squares nationwide to be surveyed in 1990.
- Sample structure: six ITE stations manage roughly 85 squares each; 128 new squares and 128 squares with prior data to be resurveyed, plus enhanced data capture in each site.
- Data capture framework: fields include land use, vegetation, landscape features, physiography, inland water/coastal features, boundary delineations, and ownership. The aim is to enable pattern detection and trend analyses across the countryside.
- Permanence and traceability: permanent markers and relocation rules are used to locate plots in future surveys; plots are tied to precise map references and field features.

## Data collection, recording and governance (FAB)

- Core data tool: Field Assessment Booklet (FAB) combines mapping, quadrat recording, and sampling. It is designed to be neat, interpretable, and machine-friendly for later analysis.
- Mapping and codes: surveyors annotate 1:10,000 scale maps with a structured set of primary and secondary codes to describe land cover, vegetation types, boundaries, buildings, and other features. A coding system allows complex mosaics to be captured efficiently (alpha-numeric codes with a defined order).
- Metadata and ownership: FAB includes ownership and contact details, histories of land ownership changes, and farmer/landowner information to support permissions and stakeholder engagement.
- Aerial photography and API: API (aerial photo interpretation) is used to identify features not on standard maps, update boundaries, and guide ground-truthing, with clear conventions to avoid misinterpretation.
- Data quality and consistency: the field handbook stresses standardised procedures and observer training to minimise recording variation, with quality control integrated into the 1990 cycle.

## Plot types and layout

- Large vegetation plots: five 200 m2 (large) quadrats per square positioned to cover diverse land-cover types; nested within-them recordings capture fine-scale vegetation composition.
- Small plots: five 2x2 m quadrats placed in natural or semi-natural vegetation to complement the large plots.
- Linear plots: up to five linear plots across hedgerows, streamsides, and roadsides to capture edge and linear habitat patterns.
- Boundary, stream, and verge plots: additional linear plots located along boundaries, streams, and road verges to capture gradients and habitat variation.
- Relocation rules: if land use changes, plots are relocated according to predefined rules to maintain comparability; if a square changes too much, alternatives are used.
- Permanent marking: marker plates are installed for each plot to facilitate future relocation and accurate re-surveys.

## Field procedures, permissions and logistics

- Permissions: multiple reasons to obtain permission (legal access, stakeholder goodwill, background information). Prior contact and formal letters help smooth access; refusals trigger predefined contingencies (e.g., replacement squares).
- Planning and logistics: six stations coordinate flexible scheduling, travel management, and leave arrangements; outreach to landowners is an ongoing process.
- Equipment: field kit includes FABs, aerial photographs, maps, weatherproof clipboards, marking tools, measurement equipment, and personal gear; some items supplied by Merlewood, others by local ITE stations or surveyors.
- General field procedure: arrive with area knowledge, secure access across the whole square, select suitable routes for full coverage, and review notes after recording to ensure completeness.

## Freshwater sampling and biodiversity (Section 7)

- Freshwater biota: two types of freshwater samples per square:
  - Flowing waters: freshwater biota sampling conducted under a separate IFE protocol.
  - Standing waters (NELUP): water beetle sampling conducted by Newcastle University, with a standardized field protocol.
- Site selection: samples drawn from various standing water types (lakes/lochs, ponds, pools in dries/ditches, calcareous and acidic flushes, sphagnum pools) to reflect square-level habitats.
- Documentation: sampling sites are mapped, labeled, and bottles are clearly identified and preserved for transport to Merlewood.

## Photography and documentation

- Photography: print film used to document vegetation plots and their relation to landmarks; plot numbers must be visible in photos; photographs support future interpretation and validation.

## Procedures after fieldwork

- End-of-day checks: ensure no features are omitted; avoid post-hoc re-entry unless damage to original materials necessitates it.
- Data and sample transport: FABs and samples should be transported to ITE stations promptly to safeguard data quality and timeliness.

## Challenges and considerations for monitoring-framework authors

- Data gaps and access: potential lack of data or restricted access to some datasets and metadata can hinder timely analysis.
- Silos and governance: organizational silos can impede data sharing; robust governance and clear data-sharing policies are needed to ensure openness while protecting sensitive information.
- Metadata quality: inadequate metadata can prevent verification and reuse; emphasis on standardised metadata at the data origin is essential.
- Data transformation: data formats often require processing; standardised field procedures and documentation help streamline integration with other data streams.
- Communication of complex findings: the framework must balance detail and clarity in communicating results to policymakers and stakeholders.
- Time-series consistency: maintaining comparability across surveys requires consistent observer training, standard protocols, and durable data structures to enable long-term trend analysis.

- In sum: the Countryside Survey 1990 provides a comprehensive, codified, field-based monitoring framework designed to detect landscape and ecological changes over time, with a strong emphasis on standardisation, data governance, and integration with remote sensing.
