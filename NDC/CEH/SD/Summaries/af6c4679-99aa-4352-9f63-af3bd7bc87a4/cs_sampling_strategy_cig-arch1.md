# The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey

- The document outlines the field survey sampling strategy for Countryside Survey up to 2007, tracing its development from the ITE Land Classification first used in 1978 and explaining how the framework supports national change detection and country-specific reporting.

## Core purpose and approach

- Establishes a stratified, random sampling framework to ensure samples are representative of Britain’s varied landscapes.
- Uses the ITE Land Classification to define strata (land classes) based on environmental attributes (e.g., altitude, geology, climate) and to guide where field surveys occur.
- Field samples are collected as a network of 1 km squares, with sampling intensity adjusted over time to meet policy and reporting needs at national and country levels (England, Scotland, Wales).

## Key concepts

- Stratification and random sampling are essential to avoid bias and ensure “typical” representation of the landscape.
- ITE Land Classification evolution:
  - 32 classes (1978) based on environmental attributes.
  - Expanded to 40 classes (1990) to enable regional and local estimations.
  - Expanded to 45 classes (2000) to accommodate separate country reporting and new data needs.
  - 2007 adjustments introduced country-unit subdivisions (e.g., Wales-specific classes) to support Wales-only reporting.
- Sampling units and layout:
  - Core unit: 1 km squares across Great Britain.
  - Initial approach included the centre square plus four surrounding squares to form a 5-square footprint for classification.
  - Sample sizes per survey grew across iterations to improve reliability of national and country-level estimates.

## Historical development and survey periods

- 1978 – Initial Land Classification and field survey
  - 1228 centre 1 km squares classified; 32 Land Classes.
  - Final sampling comprised 256 squares (8 per class) for field work.
  - National habitat estimates derived from mean habitat area per square within each Land Class.
- 1984 – Second GB land-use survey
  - Sample increased to 384 squares (12 per class); eight earlier squares retained.
  - Results published highlighting landscape changes.
- 1990 – Countryside Survey 1990 (CS1990)
  - Land Classification revised to cover all GB squares; 508 squares surveyed.
  - First link to Land Cover Map of GB from satellite data; repeat quadrats collected.
  - National estimates recalculated under the new, all-squares framework.
- 2000 – Developments for CS2000
  - Recognized need for separate country estimates (England & Wales; Scotland).
  - Class structure expanded to 40 Land Classes with reallocation of squares to better represent each country.
  - Introduction of separate country units and adjustments to sampling to maintain representation across countries.
  - A dedicated uplands module added to improve habitat estimates in upland areas.
- 2007 – Developments for CS2007 (Welsh reporting emphasis)
  - Wales required Wales-only reporting; Wales became the most intensively sampled country.
  - Land Classes subdivided into country-unit versions (creating 5 new Welsh/England subdivisions, e.g., 5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w).
  - Total strata increased to 45; 43 Welsh squares added to ensure all new classes are represented in Wales.
  - England adjustments kept to a minimum; some English classes have reduced square counts but maintained precision as per scoping studies.
  - Wales-only reporting supported by revised maps and sampling framework.

- National estimates note
  - Changing the underlying Land Classification affects comparability of national stock/area estimates.
  - For consistency, analyses may use either the latest classification (country-specific) or the original 1990 classification, depending on the reporting objective.

## Practical implications for data analysts

- Consistency and comparability:
  - When comparing across CS surveys, account for updates to the Land Classification (32 → 40 → 45 classes; country-unit subdivisions).
  - Use crosswalks or fixed classification schemes when aggregating across years to ensure meaningful trend analyses.
- Scale and granularity:
  - The framework targets both national-scale estimates and local-scale (country-specific) reporting, with intentional oversampling in Wales for Wales-only analyses.
  - The uplands module improves precision for upland habitat estimates within the England & Wales region.
- Metadata and transparency:
  - Documentation of classifications, sampling frames, and changes over time is essential for traceability and re-use.
  - Tables of class counts, sampling rates, and country allocations should accompany any analysis intended for policy or monitoring purposes.
- Data access and usability:
  - The approach emphasizes the need to track data sources and maintain accessible datasets with clear metadata, aligning with best practices in data discovery for environmental monitoring.

## Summary of outcomes

- The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey provides a coherent, evolving framework that connects land classification with a stratified sampling design to support robust, country-specific habitat estimates and UK-wide trend analysis.
- It balances methodological rigor (representativeness and statistical validity) with practical reporting needs (Welsh, English, and Scottish outputs) and acknowledges the impact of classification changes on long-term comparability.
