# The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey (up to 2007)

- Purpose and scope
  - Provides the field survey sampling strategy for Countryside Survey up to 2007.
  - The methodology builds on the ITE Land Classification, a long-running stratification framework used since 1978 to ensure representative sampling of Great Britain’s countryside.
  - Emphasises linking sampling design to data products (habitat estimates, maps, and change information) and to policy needs (country-specific reporting).

- Core concepts
  - Stratified, random sampling to avoid bias and to capture ecological variability.
  - ITE Land Classification: environmental attributes (e.g., altitude, geology, climate) define land classes used as strata.
  - 1 km square sampling units for field surveys; quadrats and habitat data collected within squares; later surveys also incorporated land-use and habitat-mapping data.
  - Indicator Species Analysis (ISA) used in early development to define vegetation-based classes, later replaced by broader environmental surrogates.

- Evolution of the Land Classification and sampling framework
  - 1978 (The Beginning)
    - First Land Classification with 32 classes derived from 1228 centre squares of a 15 x 15 km grid.
    - 8 sample squares per class, total 256 for the national survey.
  - 1984 (Second survey)
    - Increased sampling within each class to 12 squares (384 total).
  - 1990 (All Squares land classification)
    - Expanded to classify every 1 km square in GB; 508 squares surveyed.
    - Repeats of vegetation quadrats; linkage to first Land Cover Map (via satellite data).
  - 1990s (Further development and regional detail)
    - Recognised need to extend classification to all GB squares with surrogates; attempts to reclassify with broader surrogates, retaining class-based structure while acknowledging distortions in class representation.
  - 2000 (Countryside Survey CS2000)
    - Land Classification updated to support separate country estimates (England with Wales and Scotland) and to improve representation across newly defined country units.
    - Introduction of country-unit versions of land classes; aggregation/sub-division to yield about 40 strata.
    - Additional squares added (including uplands component) to ensure adequate representation of new or under-represented classes.
  - 2007 (Countryside Survey CS2007)
    - Wales-only reporting requirement drove further Wales-specific sampling adjustments.
    - Sub-division of land classes into country-unit versions (creating 5 new Welsh/England sub-classes: 5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w).
    - Strata increased to 45; 43 additional Welsh squares added; some English classes retained with smaller sample counts.
    - Wales became the most intensively sampled country unit for reporting purposes; England and Scotland sampling adjusted accordingly.

- Sampling framework and numbers (high-level)
  - CS1978: 256 squares (8 per class; 32 classes).
  - CS1984: 384 squares (12 per class; 32 classes).
  - CS1990: 508 squares (broader coverage; all GB squares classified).
  - CS2000: 569 squares (England & Wales; Scotland; revised country-unit framework; increased number of classes to about 40).
  - CS2007: 591 squares (England, Wales, Scotland; Wales-specific enhancements to support Wales-only reporting; 45 strata).
  - Country-level and total sampling context
    - England, Scotland, Wales are treated as separate reporting units with class-subdivisions and country-specific estimates.
    - Isle of Man removed from country estimates and replaced to maintain country-specific balance.
    - Wales received a substantial increase in sampled squares by 2007 for Wales-only reporting.
  - Upland module
    - An additional module in CS2000 placed an extra 30 squares in upland and marginal upland land-classes in England and Wales to improve upland habitat estimates.

- Outputs and data use
  - Outputs include habitat area estimates, land-class-based summaries, and spatial maps.
  - National estimates are derived by combining per-square habitat areas with the land-class area of each class.
  - Outputs have been used to support policy-relevant reporting and environmental status assessments; associated results are published in multiple CS reports (e.g., Countryside Survey 1990 main report, CS2000, CS2007 UK results).

- Data challenges and considerations
  - Time and effort required to “understand the ask” and align data collection with user needs across diverse teams.
  - Patchy or heterogeneous data across many formats and sources, necessitating careful data cleaning, transformation, and integration.
  - Communication of complex ecological information in accessible form for non-specialist end users.
  - Ensuring outputs are widely shared and re-used; enhancing the reach and usefulness of data products.
  - Changes in Land Classification across surveys affect consistency of national estimates; preference is often given to the latest classification for internal consistency, with caution about cross-survey comparability.
  - Balancing the need for country-specific estimates with statistical precision, leading to class subdivisions and targeted sampling in Wales and upland areas.
