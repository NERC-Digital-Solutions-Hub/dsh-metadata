# The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey

## Introduction and Core Concepts
- The field survey sampling framework for Countryside Survey (CS) has evolved over three decades and is tied to the ITE Land Classification.
- Purpose: to stratify the landscape into homogeneous units (strata) and select representative samples to detect change and estimate habitat features across Great Britain.
- Key methodological idea: use stratified random sampling to avoid bias and ensure samples cover ecological variation across land types and regions.
- Early foundation: classification and sampling developed from 1 km squares, using environmental attributes to define land classes.

## The ITE Land Classification and Stratified Sampling
- Land Classification: a multivariate system that groups land into classes (land types) based on environmental attributes (altitude, geology, climate, etc.), enabling environmental stratification for sampling.
- Sampling logic: allocate samples within each land class (strata) to capture within-class variability and enable national and regional estimates.
- Early approach used the centre square of a 15 Ã— 15 km grid across GB; surrounding squares were classified to extend the classification footprint.
- Over time, the classification was refined to better reflect regional and country-specific reporting needs (e.g., Scotland, Wales).

## Timeline of Developments

- 1978 CS (Initial Land Classification and Field Survey)
  - ISA-based classification produced 32 land classes from environmental data.
  - Sampling: 1 km squares centered within classes; eight samples per class (256 total).
  - National estimates derived by combining average habitat area per square with class land area.

- 1984 CS (Second Field Survey)
  - Sampling expanded: 12 squares per land class (384 total).
  - Continued use of the 1978 Land Classification for national estimates.

- 1990 CS (All Squares Land Classification; 3rd Field Survey)
  - Land Classification revised to classify all 1 km squares in GB; urban and sea corrections applied.
  - 508 squares surveyed; repetition of 1978 vegetation quadrats; inclusion of Land Cover Map from satellite imagery.
  - For national estimates, the revised (1990) classification was later used; some squares changed class.

- 1998 CS (Revised Land Classification; 4th Field Survey)
  - Land Classification updated to enable separate Scotland reporting; number of Land Classes increased to 40.
  - 569 squares surveyed; national estimates adjusted to reflect the revised classification.

- 2000 CS (CS2000; 5th Field Survey)
  - Addressed policy needs for separate country estimates (England & Wales vs Scotland).
  - Sub-division of classes into country-unit versions; expanded strata to 40.
  - Additional samples added to ensure adequate representation of new classes (including upland considerations).
  - Isle of Man samples replaced to align with country-unit reporting.
  - Upland module introduced to improve habitat estimates in uplands of England and Wales.

- 2007 CS (CS2007; 6th Field Survey)
  - Wales-only reporting requirements led to further Wales-specific adjustments.
  - New country-unit class subdivisions created (five new Wales/England-specific classes: 5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w).
  - Strata increased to 45; 43 additional Welsh squares added to ensure representation of new Welsh classes.
  - Some English classes reduced to four squares per class; upland sampling maintained.
  - England, Wales, and Scotland sample maps updated to reflect revised framework.

## Sampling Framework and Numbers

- CS2007: detailed class-by-class sampling adjustments; overall expansion to enable Wales- and England-specific reporting.
- Welsh adjustments: 43 additional Welsh squares; minimum six squares per Welsh class; dedicated sub-classes for Wales.
- English adjustments: some classes with four squares per class due to Welsh additions; overall precision deemed acceptable per scoping studies.
- Upland module: added 30 squares in upland and marginal upland areas (England and Wales) to improve habitat estimates in upland regions.

## Separate Country Estimates and Wales/Uplands

- Country-unit framework: Land Classes subdivided into England-with-Wales and Scotland versions to enable country-specific estimates.
- Aggregation and sub-division: to maintain adequate representation across countries, some classes were re-grouped within each country.
- Isle of Man: removed from country-unit estimates; two GB squares replaced with two England squares for country reporting.
- Wales-specific reporting: Wales required additional Welsh-class subdivisions and more Welsh samples to ensure Wales-only estimates were robust.

## Uplands Module

- An additional surveying module (CS2000 and CS2007 contexts) placed 30 squares in uplands and marginal uplands to refine habitat estimates in upland areas, acknowledging their distinct ecological characteristics.

## Data Use and National Estimates

- Changing land classifications across CS surveys affects the consistency of national estimates; cross-survey comparisons must consider classification changes.
- Preference for using the latest Land Classification for country-specific reporting, while original classifications are used for cross-survey consistency in other contexts.
- National estimates historically calculated by combining mean habitat area per square within each land class with the total land class area; later surveys aligned estimates to updated classifications and country units.
