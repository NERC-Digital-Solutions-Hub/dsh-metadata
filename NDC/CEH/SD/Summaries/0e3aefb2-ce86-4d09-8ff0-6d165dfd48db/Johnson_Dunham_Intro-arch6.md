# Moor House National Nature Reserve: Introduction

- The Moor House Reserve sits in the upland Teesdale and Westmorland Pennines, just south of Cross Fell, covering an uninhabited landscape with a field station at Moor House near Garrigill (Alston area). Access is limited by steep roads and footpaths; the Pennine bridleway crosses the area.
- Primary purpose: study upland peat moors, their ecology, and geology; the site uniquely exposes a near-complete Carboniferous stratigraphic sequence, including continuous upper Carboniferous beds best seen on the western escarpment and at Great Dun Fell.
- The Reserve provides a key natural laboratory for ecologists and geologists, offering well-preserved peat, glacial and post-glacial deposits, and several mineral veins (lead, zinc, barytes, fluorite) including the Force Burn Vein, some of which are rarely exposed elsewhere.
- Historical context: before 1951 the area was part of Appleby Castle Estates; the Nature Conservancy, local authorities, and several universities supported surveying, mapping, and ecological studies; funding supported geological mapping and lab work (notably 6-inch scale geological maps).
- Climate and environment: severe upland climate with annual rainfall around 76.3 inches, cool temperatures (roughly 55 F average in summer, 29.5 F in winter), and strong winds leading to waterlogged soils and peat formation.
- Vegetation and habitat: dominant peatland species include Calluna (heather), Eriophorum (cotton grass), and Sphagnum moss; the area supports typical Pennine moorland flora and fauna, with grazing and some tree planting for sheltered areas. Foxes are common and monitored.
- Human activity and management: grazing across the Reserve with fragile pasture; ongoing botanical and ecological enclosures; experiments to improve grazing and establish shelter belts; access restrictions to protect sites.
- Data and outputs: the project produced detailed field-based data and maps, with emphasis on self-contained data products (dashboards, pivotable data, charts) to enable user exploration; 6-inch geological maps are a notable deliverable, alongside descriptive chapters and plates.
- Acknowledgments: broad collaboration with scientists, museums, and institutes (Geological Survey, Macaulay Institute, Natural History Museum, universities, and national funders) who contributed data, analysis, and access to facilities; explicit thanks to individuals who advised on soils, peat studies, ecology, mineralogy, and field logistics.
- Key data themes described:
  - Geological Sequence: explicit separation into superficial (Recent/Post-glacial; blanket-peat, basin-peat, alluvium, glacial deposits; solifluction/boulder clays) and solid formations (Upper/Lower Carboniferous, Middle Limestone Group, Basement Series, Great Unconformity; Ordovician Skiddaw Slate Series).
  - Stratigraphic exposure: the Reserve contains almost complete Carboniferous succession, with continuous exposure on the western escarpment and at Great Dun Fell; provisions for mapping and interpretation (6-inch maps, regional figures).
  - Mineralogy and mining history: several mineral veins (lead, zinc, barytes, fluorite) exposed; some deposits never mined; veins provide ecologically and geologically valuable exposure.
  - Peat and post-glacial records: peat thickness up to around 12 feet in places; peat stratigraphy records shifts between birch forest, reed swamp, and blanket peat during Post-glacial times, with basins near Moor House offering full vegetation history.
