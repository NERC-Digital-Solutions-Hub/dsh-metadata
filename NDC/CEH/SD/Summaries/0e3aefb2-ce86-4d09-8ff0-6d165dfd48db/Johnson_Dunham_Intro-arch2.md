## Purpose and scope
- Documentation of the Moor House National Nature Reserve established post-World War II, emphasizing ecological and geological study of upland peat moors.
- Aims to provide detailed, long-term data on environmental health and natural resources through coordinated surveys, mapping, and laboratory work.
- Emphasis on integrating ecological observations with geological context to support monitoring and policy-relevant outputs.

## Data and methods
- Geological data and stratigraphy:
  - Extensive surveys of the Carboniferous sequence, including superficial and solid formations, with emphasis on peat, drift deposits, and mineral veins.
  - 6-inch scale geological mapping of the Reserve; descriptive memoirs and geological chapters produced (Chapters 12â€“17; with Chapters 12 and 13 attributed to K. C. Dunham and colleagues).
  - Use of outcrops such as Great Dun Fell and other escarpments to document transgressive veins and mineral deposits.
- Peat and soils data:
  - Detailed laboratory studies of peat and superficial deposits; attention to mineral soils and drift deposits.
  - Post-glacial peat stratigraphy informing vegetational history.
- Climate and hydrology:
  - Present-day climate characterized by high rainfall (average ~76.3 inches) and cool temperatures; wind-exposed fells influencing hydrology and peat formation.
- Biological and ecological data:
  - Vegetation including Calluna, Eriophorum, Sphagnum, and other flora; fauna typical of Pennine moorlands; ongoing grazing experiments and planting programs.
  - Evidence of long-term vegetational change via peat basins that record post-glacial history.
- Outputs and formats:
  - Publications in the Moor House memoir series; maps and plates (e.g., Plates I and III) to accompany descriptive text.
  - Data and findings shared with and hosted by collaborating institutions and museums.

## Location and setting
- Moor House Reserve located in the upland Teesdale and Westmorland Pennines, near Garrigill and Alston; characterized by rugged, remote terrain with limited access paths and a field station headquarters.
- Topography includes Pennine escarpment, ridge crests (Great Dun Fell, Little Dun Fell, Knock Fell), drumlinized and glaciated terrain, and river systems (Tees and its tributaries).

## Relevance for environmental monitoring
- Provides a long-term, multi-disciplinary baseline combining ecological, geological, and climatalogical data crucial for monitoring environmental health and policy performance over time.
- Demonstrates the value of integrated datasets (peat stratigraphy, glacial deposits, Carboniferous geology, vegetation history) for understanding past and present environmental conditions.
- The networked model of collaboration and publication supports data verification, quality assurance, and broader access to underlying data through institutional repositories.

## Key challenges and considerations for monitoring (as reflected by the document)
- Ensuring long-term value of datasets by integrating geological, peat, and ecological data rather than treating them as separate outputs.
- Facilitating access to underlying data and materials across institutions to support broader scrutiny and reuse.
