# The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey (up to 2007)
Revised and Updated from: 'The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey', C.J. Barr, September 1998. DETR CONTRACT No. CR0212
Revised by C.M.Wood CEH Lancaster, Library Avenue, Bailrigg, Lancaster. LA1 4AP
September 2011

## Overview and aim
- Documents the field survey sampling strategy for Countryside Survey up to 2007, the latest in a 30-year evolution of the ITE Land Classification.
- Emphasizes the connection between sampling framework and its predecessors; aims to ensure representative, unbiased estimates of countryside habitats and land use.

## The ITE Land Classification: origins and purpose
- Stratified sampling is essential to avoid bias and to capture variation across land types.
- The ITE Land Classification groups land into discrete classes based on environmental attributes (altitude, geology, climate, etc.) to create strata for sampling.
- Early approach used a 1 km square sample unit, enabling a nationwide stratified, random sample.

## The sampling framework: CS1978 (Initial survey)
- ISA (Indicator Species Analysis) used to classify 1 km squares into 32 land classes from environmental data for 1228 centre squares (15 x 15 km grid).
- Final classification included four surrounding squares per centre, totaling about 6039 km squares classified.
- Sampling: eight squares per land class (256 total) surveyed in 1978.
- National habitat area estimates derived by multiplying mean habitat area per square by land-class area.

## The sampling framework: CS1984 (Second field survey)
- Survey expanded to 12 km squares per land class (total 384).
- Continued use of the 1978 Land Classification for national estimates.
- Results published in Landscape Changes in Britain (1986).

## The sampling framework: CS1990 (All Squares and third field survey)
- Land Classification revised to cover all 1 km squares in GB; urban and sea corrections incorporated.
- 508 squares surveyed; repeat of 1978 vegetation quadrats and additional features recorded.
- First integration with the Land Cover Map of GB (from satellite imagery).
- National estimates based on the revised, all-squares classification.

## Developments leading to CS2000 (fourth field survey)
- Need to improve regional and local estimates; address issues from CS1990 and policy needs.
- Extended land classification with more classes (up to 40) and introduced separate Scotland reporting, with Wales and England planning considerations.
- Separate country estimates implemented: land classes subdivided into country-unit versions; some classes aggregated within countries to ensure representation.
- Additional squares allocated to ensure adequate representation of new classes across England and Wales; Isle of Man squares replaced for country-unit estimates.
- A uplands module was added to improve habitat estimates for upland areas in England and Wales.

## The sampling framework: CS2000 (fifth field survey)
- Summary table (Table 2) details GB-wide squares and country allocations; shows increased emphasis on England with Wales reporting and Scotland reporting.
- England with Wales: 13477 total GB squares; 30–33 squares per class in different configurations.
- Wales-specific adjustments increased Welsh class representation; 64 Welsh squares previously, now integrated into the Wales-focused framework.
- Additional module to survey uplands and marginal uplands to improve precision of upland habitat estimates.

## The sampling framework: CS2007 (Welsh-only reporting and Wales adjustments)
- Wales required Wales-only reporting; led to further class subdivisions into country-unit versions (e.g., 5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w).
- Resulted in 45 strata (up from 40) and 43 additional Welsh squares to ensure minimum representation in new Welsh classes.
- England adjustments necessary due to removal of Welsh squares; some English classes persist with a minimum of six squares, though a couple retain only four.
- Revised class maps and sample-square distributions shown in figures 4–7.

## Outputs, data products, and reporting
- The framework generates country-level estimates for habitat areas, change statistics, and land-use status—critical for environmental policy and planning.
- Output forms include land-class maps, National and country-specific estimates, and change statistics across survey waves.
- Tables and figures accompany the reports (e.g., CS2000 and CS2007 sample distributions, class maps).

## Data management considerations and challenges
- Balancing representativeness with growing needs for country-specific reporting (England, Wales, Scotland) and upland habitat precision.
- Computational and data-collection constraints historically limited the ability to classify every GB square at the same level of detail; surrogates and reclassification were employed.
- Changing classifications affect cross-survey comparability; national estimates can differ depending on whether latest or original classifications are used.
- The study emphasizes maintaining consistency within a survey while noting comparability across surveys.

## Practical implications for data support
- Maintain clear versioning of Land Classification across survey waves to ensure reproducible national and country estimates.
- Track changes in class definitions and country-unit subdivisions to preserve interpretability of time-series data.
- Ensure adequate sample size per class and per country-unit to support precise estimates, especially for Wales and upland habitats.
- Provide geospatial products (land-class maps) and tabular estimates to enable self-service analysis and policy use.
