# COUNTRYSIDE SURVEY 2000 QUALITY ASSURANCE EXERCISE

## Overview
- Purpose: Assess consistency and reliability of the CS2000 field programme through a Quality Assurance (QA) exercise.
- Scope: 38 of 519 squares from the 1998 survey; one quarter of each selected square resurveyed, covering 234 plots across nine plot types.
- Focus: Evaluate plot relocation efficiency, species concordance, accuracy of vegetation cover, land-cover mapping, and recording of changes since 1990; relate findings to CS1990 QA results; assess data quality for monitoring.

## Aims
- Quantify the accuracy of field recording in CS2000 and comment on change statistics.
- Examine plot relocation efficiency (location, orientation, and plate/plot recovery).
- Explain recording differences in terms of observer error, plot location, management, and seasonality.
- Compare CS2000 findings with the 1990 CS survey and its QA.
- Assess mapping accuracy (primary and qualifying land-cover codes, boundaries, and hedge features).
- Evaluate the level of change recorded since 1990 and distinguish real change from coding corrections.

## Methods and Scope
- Sampling: 38 squares (one quarter resurveyed); some squares continued from 1990 QA; plots cover quadrats and linear plots (road verges, streamsides, boundaries, arable margins).
- Relocation: Buried plates and original sketches used to relocate plots after eight years; assessors compared CS2000 relocations with 1990 baselines and QA standards.
- Data collected: 234 plots across plot types; 38 squares analyzed; comparisons of land-cover codes, boundary features, and vegetation.
- Measures of performance: plate/plot relocation success, species concordance, vegetation cover agreement, and change concordance.
- Analytical approaches: DECORANA ordination to assess direction and magnitude of change; mean axis scores and variance comparisons; concordance and efficiency metrics for primary land-cover codes, BAP codes, boundary codes, hedge codes, and land-use samples.

## Key Findings

### Plot Relocation
- Relocation success: 86.7% of plots satisfactorily relocated; 60% accurately relocated, ~25% approximately relocated, ~15% inadequately relocated.
- Plate recovery: 69.3% of plates located by assessors; overall relocation efficiency eight years on comparable to 12 months after burial in prior QA.
- Comparison to CS1990 QA: retained high relocation efficiency (~87%), indicating markers/plots can be relocated after many years with similar success.

### Species Concordance and Recording Accuracy
- Mean species per plot: CS2000 17.9 vs QA 20.4; QA tended to record more species, reflecting longer lists rather than true ecological change.
- Overall accuracy: initial assessment accuracy around 73% for CS2000 records, with discrepancies largely attributable to plot location/orientation errors.
- Nature of mismatches: substantial contributions from overlooked species, mis-identifications (especially among grasses), and mis-location/orientation of plots.
- Categories of variation (TI vs T2): Overlooked species and mis-location/orientation were major contributors; management/seasonal changes also influenced mismatches.
- Coverage of changes: DECORANA analysis shows axis 1 changes generally not biased toward direction of change; most discrepancies arise from location/orientation and cover-value differences.

### Vegetation Cover
- Cover estimates: Agreement between CS2000 and QA for cover levels generally good, but several species (notably grasses) showed significant discrepancies in cover.
- Specifics: Some taxa (e.g., Holcus lanatus, Dactylis glomerata, Anthoxanthum odoratum) displayed significant differences in cover between surveyors and assessors; seasonality influenced certain discrepancies (e.g., Anthoxanthum).

### Land-Cover Mapping and Boundaries
- Primary land-cover codes: Overall concordance high; 87.5% agreement (excluding BAPs), with high code concordance (92.0%) for primary boundary codes and 87.5% overall land-cover agreement.
- BAP codes: 77.4% concordance; more challenging due to broader habitat classifications and boundary delineations.
- Boundary features: Higher-level consistency for boundary types; primary boundary concordance 85.0% with code concordance 92.0%; hedge configurations introduced new codes with limited success; some inconsistencies in hedge and boundary coding.
- Boundary heights and stockproof status: High agreement (boundary height 89.2%; stockproof 83.8%), indicating reliable field recording of boundary attributes.
- Change in land-use codes: Overall concordance for change recording around 51.4%; many changes noted by surveyors were not substantiated by QA assessors, and a substantial proportion of real changes since 1990 were missed or misrecorded.

### Hedge Diversity and Arable Plots
- Hedge diversity (19 plots): Total species 124; common species 92; agreement 74.2%; surveyor efficiency 77.4%; mean species per hedge plot 5.16 CS2000 vs 5.89 QA.
- Arable plots (5 plots): Very limited data; concordance was extremely poor, with seasonality and unfamiliarity with ruderal species contributing to mis-records.

### Changes in Frequency of Key Species
- Ranking of common species largely similar between CS2000 and QA, but with notable discrepancies for several grasses and forbs.
- Over-recording of some grasses (e.g., Poa trivialis) and under-recording of others (e.g., Agrostis capillaris, Elymus repens) observed; moss recording generally poor, reducing overall concordance.

### Change and Axis Analysis
- DECORANA axis 1: No significant directional bias between CS2000 and QA means (CS2000 387.8 vs QA 392.0; Bartlett tests non-significant).
- Plot-type and landclass analysis: No evidence of systematic bias across landclasses; individual plot types showed similar patterns of change with no consistent directional shift.

## Data Quality and Governance Implications for Monitoring Frameworks
- Strengths:
  - High reliability in mapping components (primary land-cover and boundary codes) and relatively robust plot relocation framework.
  - Consistent process for QA comparisons and use of standardized protocols across multiple plot types and land classes.
- Challenges:
  - Data gaps and access to high-quality metadata; a significant burden around locating and aligning plots across years.
  - Substantial non-concordance due to plot location/orientation errors and overlooked species, especially in grass-dominated plots.
  - Change recording is inconsistent; many real changes since 1990 were missed or not substantiated, indicating gaps in documenting and verifying change.
  - Arable plots are particularly unreliable under current protocols; seasonality and unfamiliarity with ruderal taxa reduce accuracy.
  - New coding schemes (e.g., hedge/Boundary codes, BAP classifications) require clearer guidance and training to improve consistency.
- Implications for monitoring frameworks:
  - Emphasize rigorous plot-marking, precise relocation protocols, and explicit documentation of plot orientation.
  - Invest in metadata completeness and data provenance to support reproducibility and audit trails.
  - Implement targeted training to reduce mis-identifications (especially grasses) and to improve consistency in cover estimation.
  - Strengthen procedures for recording and validating changes, possibly integrating real-time field verification or joint field reviews.
  - Ensure arable/ruderal plotting protocols are robust, with sufficient visits to account for seasonal variation.
  - Maintain data governance practices to support sharing and reuse, addressing potential barriers noted in other monitoring frameworks.

## Takeaways for Monitoring Framework Authors
- To inform policy and future decisions, QA in large-scale environmental surveys should target:
  - Location and orientation accuracy as foundational data quality drivers.
  - Transparent reporting of observer effects, including misidentifications and coverage biases.
  - Robust change-detection recording with clear criteria for when a change is real versus a coding correction.
  - Mapping code concordance as a strong proxy for data usability in policy evaluation.
  - Systematic handling of seasonality and plot-type-specific challenges (notably arable/ruderal contexts).
- The CS2000 QA demonstrates that while overall data quality can be high for core mapping and location tasks, meaningful improvements are needed in species-level recording, change documentation, and arable/ruderal work to strengthen environmental monitoring frameworks.
