## Implications for monitoring frameworks
- Importance of robust, well-documented data provenance:
  - Field installation details (orientation, depth offsets) directly affect interpretation of readings.
  - Regular maintenance and hardware integrity checks are critical to data continuity.
- Handling data gaps and corrections:
  - Anticipate data loss from missed downloads, logger timeouts, and environmental interference; implement strategies to minimize gaps (e.g., automated backups, redundancy, and explicit gap annotations).
  - Maintain clear records of offsets and processing changes to ensure consistent time series alignment.
- Metadata richness and governance:
  - Comprehensive metadata around instrumentation, calibration, and data processing steps is essential to enable policy-relevant analysis.
  - Establish data governance processes to manage sharing, metadata standards, and version control.
- Data quality as a policy-informing factor:
  - Recognize how animal interference, environmental conditions, and equipment failures can bias readings; plan monitoring designs that mitigate these risks.
