# The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey

- The field survey sampling strategy for Countryside Survey 2007 is the latest in a 30-year development of the ITE Land Classification, which underpins the national ecological sampling framework.
- The methodology prioritizes representative sampling through stratified random design, using land classes to structure the GB landscape and ensure coverage of ecological variation across regions and countries.
- The document traces the evolution from early 1 km square sampling and Indicator Species Analysis (ISA) to progressively refined land classifications and country-specific reporting needs.

## The ITE Land Classification: Evolution and Concept

- Origin: Land classification built from environmental attributes (e.g., altitude, geology, climate) using a multivariate approach (ISA) on quadrat-scale data scaled to 1 km squares.
- 1978 baseline: Centre squares of a 15 x 15 km grid classified into 32 land classes; surrounding four squares classified to extend context.
- Purpose: Provide an objective stratification framework to sample representative ecological units across Great Britain.
- Class progression:
  - 32 classes (early version) → expanded over time to capture more detailed land types.
  - 1990: Land Classification revised to classify all GB squares (all-squares classification) but with some changes to maintain continuity.
  - 2000 (CS2000): Land Classes expanded to 40 to support Scotland reporting and policy needs; introduced separate country estimates.
  - 2007 (CS2007): Welsh-specific reporting required further subdivisions, increasing to 45 classes and enabling Wales-only estimates.

## The Sampling Framework Across Surveys

- 1978 (Initial Survey):
  - 8 samples per Land Class; total of 256 1 km squares.
  - Focus on vegetation quadrats and soils; habitat areas estimated by class proportions.
- 1984 (Second GB Survey):
  - Sample size increased by 50% to 12 squares per class (total 384).
  - Results published later; emphasized land use and habitat mapping.
- 1990 (CS1990; All Squares Land Classification):
  - Revised classification to cover all GB squares; 508 squares surveyed.
  - Added land cover mapping; repeat of 1978 quadrats.
- 2000 (CS2000):
  - UK-wide framework adjusted for separate England with Wales and Scotland reporting.
  - Sub-divided classes into country-unit versions; total strata increased to 40.
  - Added 19 extra squares to ensure adequate representation of new classes; upland habitat focus added via an uplands module.
- 2007 (CS2007):
  - Welsh-only reporting drove: five new Welsh sub-classes (5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w); increased Wales sampling to ensure minimum representation.
  - England adjustments: no added Welsh squares; two classes (5e, 15e) remained with 4 squares.
  - Total strata increased to 45; 43 additional Welsh squares allocated; Wales had the most intensive sampling relative to land area.
  - Upland module continued to improve accuracy for upland habitat estimates.

## Wales, England, Scotland: Country-Specific Adjustments

- Separate country estimates:
  - England with Wales and Scotland reporting, using country-specific sampling frames.
  - Isles of Man squares removed/repurposed to maintain country-specific integrity.
- Wales:
  - Welsh class sub-divisions created (5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w); minimum 6 squares per Welsh class.
  - 43 additional Welsh squares added to achieve adequate representation.
- England:
  - No additional English squares in some classes; two classes (5e, 15e) have only 4 squares each.
- Scotland:
  - Scotland reporting maintained within its own class framework; separate counts and maps applied.
- Upland module:
  - An additional 30 upland squares were added to bolster habitat estimates in uplands of England and Wales.

## Summary of Key Implications

- Countryside Survey’s sampling strategy is deeply tied to the ITE Land Classification, evolving to accommodate national and country-specific reporting needs (England, Scotland, Wales).
- Stratified random sampling at the 1 km square level, guided by a multi-class land classification, enables robust national and regional habitat estimates.
- The strategy continuously adjusts class structure and sample allocation to balance representation, statistical precision, and policy-relevant reporting (notably Wales-only and upland habitat estimates).
- Data interpretation and national estimates can be sensitive to the underlying land classification; consistency considerations and cross-year comparability are explicitly acknowledged.
