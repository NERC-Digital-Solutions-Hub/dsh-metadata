# The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey (up to 2007)

- Purpose and scope
  - Documents the field survey sampling strategy for Countryside Survey up to 2007.
  - Traces the evolution of the ITE Land Classification and its use as a stratification framework for GB-wide ecological surveys over ~30 years.
  - Aims to ensure representative, unbiased samples to support time-series assessment of habitats and land use.

- Core concepts for data governance
  - Stratified, random sampling to ensure representative coverage of ecological variation across GB.
  - Use of the ITE Land Classification to define strata (land classes) with environmental attributes (altitude, geology, climate, etc.).
  - Time-series continuity: sampling framework is tightly linked to previous surveys to enable change detection.

- The ITE Land Classification: origins and purpose
  - Developed in the 1970s to classify land into discrete classes based on environmental attributes.
  - Initially created from a subset of 1 km squares (center squares of a 15x15 km grid); 1228 center squares classified, plus surrounding squares for context.
  - The classification underpins the sampling framework and subsequent national habitat estimates.

- Chronology of sampling and land classification developments
  - 1978 (First GB ecological survey)
    - ISA (Indicator Species Analysis) used to derive 32 land classes from 1228 center squares.
    - 8 sample squares per class (total 256) surveyed in field.
    - National estimates: mean habitat area per square in each class Ã— area of that class.
  - 1984 (Second GB land-use survey)
    - Framework retained, but total sampled squares increased to 12 per class (total 384).
  - 1990 (All Squares Land Classification; CS1990)
    - Updated to classify every GB 1 km square; 508 squares surveyed.
    - Continued field survey with the expanded classification; linked to the first Land Cover Map of GB.
  - 1998 (Revised Land Classification; CS1990-based reporting)
    - Expansion to cover Scotland reporting; land classes increased to 40.
    - 569 squares surveyed; results published with new classification.
  - 2000 (CS2000: separate country estimates)
    - Recognized need for England & Wales vs Scotland reporting; country-based estimates introduced.
    - Land Classes subdivided into country-unit versions; total strata increased to 40.
    - 19 additional squares added to ensure representation of new classes; uplands module added to improve upland habitat estimates.
  - 2007 (CS2007: Wales-focused refinements)
    - Wales required Wales-only reporting; added Welsh sub-divisions (5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w) creating 45 strata.
    - 43 additional Welsh squares surveyed; England adjusted (some classes left with only 4 squares) to maintain consistency and precision.
    - Wales becomes the most intensively sampled country unit; updated maps show England, Wales, and Scotland sampling framework.

- Sampling framework and outputs
  - Strata and sample distribution
    - The land-class framework is gradually subdivided to accommodate country-specific reporting.
    - Total GB sampling scales from 32 to 40 (CS2000) and to 45 (CS2007) strata with country-unit considerations.
  - Country-specific estimates
    - England & Wales and Scotland estimates derived from country-specific samples, using subdivided land classes and reweighted representations where needed.
    - Isle of Man samples removed from country-unit estimates; replaced to maintain representativeness.
  - Uplands module (CS2000)
    - Added 30 upland squares to improve habitat estimates in upland regions of England and Wales.
  - Welsh-specific refinements (CS2007)
    - Welsh sub-classes created and representation increased to ensure robust Wales-only reporting.

- Data products and documentation
  - Maps and tables illustrating revised land-class frameworks and sample square distributions (e.g., figures showing England/Wales/Scotland classifications and sampling squares).
  - Tables summarizing the numbers of squares surveyed per survey year and per land-class stratum.
  - Note on national estimates: changing land-class classifications affects cross-survey consistency; the preferred approach is to use the latest classification for country estimates while maintaining cross-survey consistency where possible.

- Key considerations for data stewardship
  - Provenance and methodological traceability
    - Detailed historical lineage of land classifications and sampling decisions across CS1978, CS1984, CS1990, CS2000, and CS2007.
  - Data comparability and versioning
    - Explicit note that reclassification can alter GB-wide estimates; documentation clarifies which classification was used for which survey when computing national estimates.
  - Metadata and access
    - Rich documentation accompanies the data (class descriptions, sampling procedures, and country-unit adaptations) to support reuse and governance.
  - Data updates and governance
    - Framework accommodates ongoing updates (e.g., uplands module) and country-specific reporting needs, while preserving a coherent time-series structure.

- Challenges and considerations highlighted
  - Incomplete picture of user needs and priorities at times.
  - Timeliness of data delivery from various administrative contexts.
  - Ensuring met standards and metadata for all land-class subdivisions across countries.
  - Interoperability across evolving classifications and country-unit reporting requirements.
  - Maintaining robust representation for Wales, Scotland, and England in the face of devolution and policy needs.
  - Balancing precision and sample size as class definitions become more granular (e.g., 5w, 6w, etc.).

- Endnotes and references
  - Acknowledgements and further reading highlight contributing researchers, statistical guidance, and published results for CS1990, CS2000, and CS2007.
  - The document provides a concise history and justification for continuing methodological evolution to support reliable, country-specific environmental reporting.

- Overall takeaway for data stewards
  - The Countryside Survey sampling strategy demonstrates how a long-running ecological data program evolves a stratified sampling framework and land-class system to maintain representativeness, enable time-series analyses, and meet country-specific reporting needs, all while documenting methodological changes to preserve data integrity and governance.
