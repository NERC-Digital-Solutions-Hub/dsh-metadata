# FOREST STRUCTURE, COMPOSITION, AND BIRD COUNTS IN WINDBLOWN AND CONTROL SITES IN KIELDER FOREST, NORTHUMBERLAND, UK, MAYJULY 2022.

## Overview
- Examines the impact of Storm Arwen (Nov 2021) on forest structure and biodiversity in Kielder Forest using a paired controlâ€“impact design (12 pairs, 24 sites).
- Survey scope includes forest structure, ground-layer vegetation, plant species, and bird counts.

## Experimental design
- Paired design with windblown (impact) and nearby control patches to compare post-storm effects.
- Site selection aimed to balance accessibility, researcher safety, and environmental representativeness.

## Field protocols and measurements
- Transects and plots:
  - One 100-meter transect per site.
  - Three 10x10-meter plots along each transect (at 0-10 m, 50-60 m, and 90-100 m).
  - Three 2x2-meter quadrats at 0-2 m, 50-52 m, and 90-92 m.
- Fallen trees:
  - Counted along the transect; perimeter measured three times for volume estimation.
  - Recorded status (flat on ground vs. leaning) and life status (dead vs. alive).
- Vegetation and ground cover:
  - Visual estimates of grasses, shrubs, ferns, mosses, fungi, and lichens using the Braun-Blanquet scale within 2x2 m quadrats.
  - Oppose to random placement to ensure representativeness of transect sections.
- Plant identification:
  - Field guides and keys used; occasional photographic aid with iNaturalist.
- Species data:
  - Plant species inventoried within 2x2 m quadrats.

## Randomization and sampling design
- Plot and quadrat placement within transects randomized to left/right sides to ensure unbiased sampling.
- Sequential side placement across transects maintained balance.

## Data quality, metadata, and identification
- Species identifications supported by standardized references and iNaturalist photography when needed.
- Measurements and protocols described clearly to facilitate replication and auditability.

## Relevance for monitoring frameworks and policy decisions
- Provides a replicable, multi-taxa monitoring framework (tree structure, ground vegetation, and birds) suitable for assessing storm-related ecological impacts.
- Enables robust policy-relevant indicators:
  - Coarse woody debris volume (from fallen trees)
  - Tree density and DBH distribution (from 10x10 m plots)
  - Ground-layer vegetation cover and diversity (Braun-Blanquet categories and species inventories)
  - Bird community response from point counts
- The paired design supports causal inference about storm effects and can inform restoration priorities, resilience assessments, and post-disturbance management.
