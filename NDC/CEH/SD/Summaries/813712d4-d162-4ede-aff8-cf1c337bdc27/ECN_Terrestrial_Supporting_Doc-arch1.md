# Environmental Change Network Terrestrial Site Locations

- Global context: Recognition of widespread environmental changes driven by climate, atmospheric composition, and land use; growing UK and international monitoring efforts to detect, understand, and predict changes.
- UK monitoring landscape: Decentralised efforts across government, industry, academia, and NGOs; aim to harmonise data for reliable national and international comparisons; data increasingly stored in environmental data centres and GIS.
- Rationale for ECN: Long-term, integrated monitoring across UK terrestrial ecosystems to detect environmental changes, disentangle drivers, and provide uniform, comparable datasets for research and policy.

## ECN objectives

- Obtain uniform, comparable long-term datasets at selected sites by measuring core environmental variables at regular intervals.
- Integrate and analyse these datasets to identify changes and understand their causes.
- Make long-term datasets available to support research and prediction of future changes.
- Provide representative sites with robust instrumentation and reliable information for research purposes.

## ECN design and methodological approach

- Ecological monitoring scope: Focus on changes in selected biota in addition to physical and chemical environment; aim for interpretable, comparable, and repeatable measurements across sites and time.
- Core vs priority measurements: Identify a core set of measurements to be taken at all sites; priority additional measurements depend on funding and feasibility.
- Data protocols and quality control: Establish clear sampling, recording, and analytical protocols; implement data management procedures; ensure cross-site comparability.
- Statistical and analytical planning: Involve statisticians early to address site placement, replication, data storage, and analysis strategies; emphasize simple, robust analyses with cautious use of complex models; apply exploratory data analysis and cross-validation.
- Sectoral integration: Where possible, adopt sampling designs from ongoing sectoral monitoring to enable broader regional and national analyses.
- Data handling: Central coordination unit standardises procedures, coordinates data collection and management, and maintains metadata for discoverability.

## Development and site selection

- Stepwise implementation: Focus first on terrestrial sites, with freshwater (rivers and lakes) networks to follow; each agency contributes sites and resources.
- Site selection criteria for the terrestrial spine (24 target sites considered; 11 selected in practice):
  - Broad geographical distribution across UK environments.
  - Reasonable long-term physical and financial security.
  - Known history of consistent management and data records (10+ years preferred).
  - Sufficient site size and replication potential to allow further experiments.
- Governance and funding realities: Long-term commitment challenges due to multi-agency funding; initial backbone chosen to maximise representativeness within budget.

## Terrestrial ECN network sites

- Alice Holt Forest, Surrey, England
  - Sponsor: Forestry Commission
  - Key features: Longstanding ecological and atmospheric monitoring; EU site for monitoring atmospheric deposition effects on trees; diverse forest structure and management research.
- Drayton, Warwickshire, England
  - Sponsor: Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food
  - Key features: Intensive mixed farming system; long-running experiments on weed, pest, and disease control; pesticide and nitrogen input studies; runoff pollution investigations.
- Glensaugh, Grampian Region, Scotland
  - Sponsor: Scottish Office, Agriculture, Environment & Fisheries Department
  - Key features: Large, diverse upland site with long-term resource management data; research on agroforestry, vegetation dynamics, ruminant foraging, and nutrient/pollutant dynamics.
- Hillsborough, Co Down, Northern Ireland
  - Sponsor: Department of Agriculture for Northern Ireland
  - Key features: Grassland-dominated farmland with adjoining forest and a notable bog/grassland interface; diverse agricultural and environmental research, including forage and soil studies.
- Moor House – Upper Teesdale, Cumbria and Durham, England
  - Sponsors: Natural Environmental Research Council and English Nature
  - Key features: UNESCO Biosphere Reserve; upland arctic/alpine plant communities; extensive long-term experiments on grazing, burning, drainage, and climate-related processes; TIGER programme location.
- North Wyke, Devon, England
  - Sponsor: Biotechnology & Biological Sciences Research Council
  - Key features: Grassland and grazing systems research; nutrient cycling (N, P) through farm to environment; low-input grassland farming and greenhouse gas flux studies; long-term meteorological records.
- Porton Down, Wiltshire, England
  - Sponsor: Ministry of Defence
  - Key features: Chalk downland with rare habitats; extensive survey of unique grassland communities and juniper scrub; high biodiversity with notable invertebrate populations; historic restricted-use military landscape.
- Rothamsted, Hertfordshire, England
  - Sponsor: Biotechnology & Biological Sciences Research Council
  - Key features: World’s oldest agricultural research station; long-running classical experiments (e.g., Broadbalk, Park Grass); 100+ years of soil, crop, and atmospheric data; continuous insect monitoring network.
- Sourhope, Borders Region, Scotland
  - Sponsor: Scottish Office, Agriculture, Environment & Fisheries Department
  - Key features: Highland grazing system with sheep and cashmere goats; long-term soil fertility and vegetation studies; diverse land uses from rough grazing to silage production; emphasis on semi-fine wool and cashmere production.
- Wytham, Oxfordshire, England
  - Sponsor: Natural Environmental Research Council
  - Key features: Sage historic ecology site operated by University of Oxford; TIGER programme focus on climate change impacts on woodland and grassland; long-term records of birds, mammals, and invertebrates; research collaborations with multiple disciplines.
- Y Wydfa/Snowdon, Gwynedd, Wales
  - Sponsors: Countryside Council for Wales and Welsh Office
  - Key features: Rugged mountainous site including Snowdon summit; extensive altitudinal gradient; rich arctic/alpine flora and fauna; long-running grazing and vegetation studies; diverse calcicolous and blanket mire communities.

- Note: A parallel freshwater network (rivers and lakes) started in 1995, with data integrated where applicable; the terrestrial spine is designed to be complemented by these freshwater sites for a holistic environmental-change view.

## Operation and governance

- Steering and management: ECN Steering Committee (high-level policy decisions); ECN Executive Committee (ECNEX) handles urgent decisions; Statistics and Technical Advisory Groups (STAG) provide technical and statistical guidance (now amalgamated).
- Central coordination: NERC oversees day-to-day management via the Central Coordination Unit (CCU); four full-time staff manage standardisation, data collection, and data management.
- Site-level data handling: Each site has a sponsoring agency and a Site Manager responsible for local data collection, initial processing, and transmission of data to the ECN Data Manager in standardized formats.

## Data, quality, and dissemination

- Emphasis on uniform protocols, traceable data sources, and accessible metadata to enable data discovery and reuse.
- Integration with sectoral monitoring schemes enhances national-scale interpretation and policy relevance.
- Long-term, curated datasets intended for research use, policy support, and predictive modelling of environmental change.
