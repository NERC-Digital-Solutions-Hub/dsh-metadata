# The Sampling Strategy for Countryside Survey (up to 2007)

- Purpose and scope
  - Describes the field survey sampling framework for Countryside Survey up to 2007, as the latest development of the ITE Land Classification used to stratify the national survey.
  - Aims to enable time-series understanding of habitat areas and change across Great Britain, with links to prior and subsequent surveys.
  - Emphasizes that the methodology is tightly integrated with the evolving ITE Land Classification, which underpins sample stratification and national estimates.

- Evolution of the ITE Land Classification and sampling framework
  - 1978 (Initial Land Classification)
    - Use of Indicator Species Analysis (ISA) to classify 1 km squares into 32 land classes from environmental attributes.
    - Centre squares of a 15 x 15 km grid (1228 squares classified; plus surrounding squares classified for a total of 6039 km squares).
    - Sampling: 8 squares per land class (256 total) for national habitat area estimates.
  - 1984 (Second Field Survey)
    - Survey expanded to 12 squares per land class (total 384); estimates still based on the 1978 classification.
  - 1990 (All Squares Land Classification)
    - Land Classification revised to classify all GB squares; 508 squares surveyed.
    - Introduction of Land Cover Map from satellite imagery; more comprehensive feature recording.
    - Results published as Countryside Survey 1990 main report.
  - 1998 (Revised Land Classification)
    - Classification extended to allow separate Scottish reporting; number of land classes increased to 40.
    - Field survey: 569 squares; results published in 2000 accounting.
  - 2000 (Countryside Survey 2000; CS2000)
    - Motive to enable country-specific reporting (England & Wales; Scotland) with adequate representation by country.
    - Land Classes subdivided into country-unit versions; total strata increased to 40.
    - Additional 19 squares allocated; further adjustments to ensure representation across new classes; upland habitat module introduced to improve accuracy for upland estimates.
  - Wales-focused adjustments (CS2007 preparation)
    - Welsh-only reporting needs led to further class subdivisions into Wales-specific versions (creating 5 new Welsh classes: 5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w).
    - Strata increased to 45; 43 additional squares allocated to Wales to ensure minimum representation per Welsh class.
    - England adjustments: no additional Welsh squares allocated; two English classes with fewer squares (4) deemed acceptable per scoping study.
  - 2007 (Countryside Survey 2007; CS2007)
    - Welsh country-unit reporting strengthened; framework includes Wales-specific classes and adjusted sample allocation.
    - Detailed maps show revised England-Wales-Scotland Land Class framework and sample squares.

- Sampling design and data structure
  - Stratified random sampling across Land Classes to ensure representative coverage of GB ecological variation.
  - For cross-year comparability, national estimates are sensitive to classification changes; the report discusses approaches to maintain consistency.
  - Separate country estimates introduced to reflect policy and regulatory needs of England with Wales, Scotland, and later Wales-specific reporting.
  - Upland modules added to CS2000 and CS2007 to improve habitat estimation accuracy in upland regions.

- Outputs, format, and documentation
  - Tables and figures present detailed counts of sample squares by year, country, and land-class strata.
  - Maps illustrating classified land classes and survey squares across GB, England with Wales, and Scotland.
  - Key publications linked to each cycle (e.g., CS1990 main report; CS2000 and 2007 documentation; UK results reports).
  - Note on national estimates: shifting between classifications affects consistency; preference to use the latest classification for GB-wide estimates while maintaining cross-survey comparability.

- Data governance and stewardship implications
  - Data stewards should manage multiple classification versions and document the lineage of squares and strata across surveys.
  - Metadata must capture country-unit distinctions (England & Wales, Scotland) and the corresponding land-class subdivisions (including Welsh-specific classes).
  - When disseminating time-series habitat estimates, clearly state which Land Classification version was used and how changes in classification impact comparability.
  - Ensure traceability of upland module data and its integration with core survey outputs.

- Key challenges and considerations for data management
  - Representativeness and sampling adequacy in Wales and Scotland, and across expanding land-class sets.
  - Balancing the need for country-specific policy reporting with maintaining cross-year comparability.
  - Managing the increasing number of strata (from 32 to 40, then to 45) and the resulting sample sizes per class.
  - Impact of reclassification on national estimates and the need for transparent methodology notes.

- Practical implications for users and stakeholders
  - The framework provides robust, policy-relevant habitat area estimates and change statistics across GB, with clearly defined country units.
  - Users should reference the appropriate Land Classification version when interpreting estimates (and rely on documented cross-walks where available).
  - The upland modules and Wales-specific adjustments reflect evolving user needs for more granular, region-specific environmental reporting.
