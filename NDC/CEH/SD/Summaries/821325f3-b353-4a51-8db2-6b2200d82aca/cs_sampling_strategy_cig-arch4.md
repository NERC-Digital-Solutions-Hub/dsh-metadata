## Key concepts and purpose

- The sampling framework is built on stratified random sampling to avoid bias and ensure representativeness across Great Britain.
- The ITE Land Classification groups land into discrete classes based on environmental attributes, serving as the basis for sampling strata and for estimating national and regional habitat and land-use features.
- Over time, classifications were revised to better support country-specific reporting (England, Wales, Scotland) and to improve precision for upland habitats and other policy-relevant units.
- Data quality considerations include how changes in classification affect consistency of national estimates and decisions about which classification to use for different survey periods.

## Evolution of the ITE Land Classification and sampling framework

- Early concept (pre-1978) and the importance of stratification to obtain representative samples of ecological variation.
- Original Land Classification (mid-1970s):
  - 1 km squares used as sampling units.
  - 32 Land Classes created by classifying centre squares (1228 classified; plus surrounding squares for a total of 6039).
  - 8 samples per class, total of 256 squares, surveyed for vegetation and soils.
- Countryside Survey 1984:
  - Sampling increased to 12 squares per class (total 384).
  - Framework still based on the 1978 Land Classification; results published later.
- Countryside Survey 1990 (CS1990):
  - Land Classification revised to cover all 1 km squares in GB (more comprehensive, with urban/sea corrections).
  - 508 squares surveyed; repetition of 1984 quadrats; inclusion of a Land Cover Map from satellite imagery.
  - Resulted in a transition from the original grid-based approach to a broader all-squares framework.
- Developments leading to CS1990 and beyond:
  - Recognition that GB-wide classification needed to be extended to classify every square, with surrogates for climatic/geologic factors when full data were not feasible.
  - Comparisons between the original 1228-square classification and new attempts showed 62% exact correspondence, with most misclassifications staying within neighboring classes; overall class means were preserved.
- Countryside Survey 2000 (CS2000):
  - Introduced separate country estimates (England+Wales vs Scotland) due to devolved policy needs.
  - Land Classes subdivided into country-unit versions (creating 40 strata) and aggregated where necessary to maintain representation.
  - Additional squares added to ensure adequate representation of new classes; upland habitat sampling module added to improve precision for upland estimates.
  - Wales received more intensive sampling to support Wales-only reporting; adjustments in England followed to maintain comparability.
- Welsh-only reporting and CS2007:
  - Wales required independent Welsh reporting; adjustments included new country-unit subdivisions (5 new Welsh sub-classes: 5w, 6w, 7w, 15w, 18w) and an expanded 45 strata framework.
  - Wales gained additional squares to ensure minimum representation in new Welsh classes; England adjustments were made to maintain overall balance.
  - The England, Wales, Scotland sampling maps and class distributions were updated to reflect these changes.

## Summary of survey steps and country-specific adaptations

- CS1978 (Initial Land Classification and Field Survey):
  - 32 classes from 1228 centre squares; 8 squares per class (256 total) surveyed.
  - National habitat estimates produced by combining mean habitat area per square with the area of each Land Class.
- CS1984 (Second Field Survey):
  - 12 squares per class (384 total); continued use of the 1978 classification for national estimates.
- CS1990 (All Squares Land Classification and Third Field Survey):
  - Land Classification updated to include all GB squares; 508 squares surveyed.
  - Inclusion of a Land Cover Map; repeat of 1984 quadrats; urban and sea corrections applied.
  - National estimates calculated using the 1990 classification (with some square-class changes observed).
- CS2000 (Revised Land Classification and Fourth Field Survey):
  - Introduction of separate country estimates (England+Wales vs Scotland) with country-specific class subdivisions (40 strata).
  - 19 additional squares added to ensure representation of new classes; 11 additional squares to balance England and Wales.
  - A dedicated upland habitat module was added to improve upland estimate precision.
- CS2007 (Revised Land Classification and Fifth Field Survey):
  - Wales-only reporting requirement led to Wales-focused sampling adjustments (5 new Welsh sub-classes; total 45 strata).
  - Additional Welsh squares added (minimum of 6 per Welsh class); two Welsh classes in England retained with 4 squares per class due to relative precision considerations.
  - Wales received increased sampling intensity to support country-specific reporting, with updates to the England/Scotland framework accordingly.

## Data quality, reporting, and national estimates

- National estimates can be produced using either the latest country-specific Land Classification or the original 1990 classification; the 2006 scoping study favored using the latest country-specific classification for consistency within a country, while a single cross-survey approach favored consistency across surveys.
